The Prayer of the Fraction

Summary of the Previous Lesson:

The prayer of the Commemoration of the Departed (Al-Tarheem) in the Divine Liturgy comes after the prayer of the Assembly of Saints (Al-Magma'). Abouna begins it inaudibly (Sirran) by placing a hand of incense in the censer and saying:

"Remember also, O Lord, those who have fallen asleep and reposed in the priesthood, and those in every rank of the laity... etc."

I explained this in detail last time. The Tarheem is the revival of the memory of the departure of our fathers, siblings, and all beloved ones who have fallen asleep. Therefore, we pray for them, asking for mercy and repose (Niyaha), just as they also pray for us. The Tarheem is a firm doctrine and faith of the Church regarding the communion of saints and the unity of the remaining and departed members of the Church. As He says in His Holy Gospel: "For He is not the God of the dead but of the living, for all live to Him." (Luke 20:38).

The Tarheem reminds us of eternal life when we remember those who have departed, because this is our hope and our faith: that there is eternal life with the Lord Jesus Christ. If we lost this hope, we would be the most miserable of people, and we would say, like our teacher Paul the Apostle: "If the dead do not rise, 'Let us eat and drink, for tomorrow we die!'" (1 Corinthians 15:32).

The Tarheem was mentioned in the Liturgy of Saint Cyril (the Liturgy of Mark the Apostle), and the rest of the Liturgies adopted it. The prayer of the Tarheem begins when Abouna says:

"Those, O Lord, and everyone whose names we have mentioned, and those whom we have not mentioned; those who are in the mind of each one of us, and those who are not in our minds; who have fallen asleep and reposed in the faith of Christ."

Abouna begins to mention the names present before him on the Altar and places incense in the censer. He completes the prayer and says: "Graciously accord, O Lord, to repose all their souls in the bosom of our holy fathers Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob... etc."

The Tarheem is for those who have departed. But what is our position, we who are living? That is why after the Tarheem, Abouna raises his hands and says:

"Those, O Lord, whose souls You have taken, repose them in the Paradise of Joy, and we who are sojourners in this place, keep us in Your faith, and grant us Your peace unto the end."

Of course, the response of the people when they say: "As it was, and shall be, from generation to generation, and unto the ages of all ages. Amen." This is a response that demonstrates the life of continuity in the Church according to apostolic tradition (Tasbeha Rasouli), which will remain until the end of days. That is why our Church is strong and steadfast by the power of her God.

After this beautiful prayer, which is "And guide us to Your kingdom," you notice that Abouna stands to the side of the Altar, so the Paten (Siniya) containing the Body of our Lord Jesus Christ and the covered Chalice (Kass) become visible. He bows his head and says: "Peace be with all of you," without making the sign of the cross. Because this peace comes from the Lord of Glory, Jesus Christ, on the lips of Abouna to everyone present in the church. Any greeting of peace after this is from the Lord of Glory. (This is a note you must pay attention to).

The Sixth Station: The Prayer of the Fraction (Salat Al-Qisma)

God willing and if we live, the Prayer of the Fraction will take more than one week to explain because the ritual and spiritual explanation is very important.

Aside from that, before I begin, I liked a saying by our master Anba Severus Ibn al-Muqaffa [1] when he said:

"The Eucharist is like the cluster of grapes brought by Joshua the son of Nun and Caleb the son of Jephunneh from the Promised Land, to create longing in the people and encourage them in their struggle. This is an encouragement for the people to continue the struggle when they first taste the fruits of the Promised Land."

The Promised Land is the Heavenly Jerusalem. (Because the Liturgy and the Church are a picture of the Heavenly Jerusalem on earth).

The Prayer of the Fraction is the prayer of distributing the portion and the inheritance. In the Prayer of the Fraction, the Lord Jesus Christ gives a portion and an inheritance to all who partake of Him, but at the same time, He is not divided. Because every part or particle (Gawhara) that the communicant receives is the complete Body. That is why the Prayer of the Fraction holds a great and awe-inspiring place in the Divine Liturgy. (In the past, the deacons would leave the Sanctuary / Al-Haykal before the Fraction, and only one deacon would remain with Abouna).

In reality, the breaking of the bread begins in the Prayers of Institution (Al-Salawat Al-Ta'sisiyya), which I explained previously. Let me remind you: it is when Abouna begins to pray and say: "He rose from the dead on the third day, ascended into the heavens... etc." This prayer ends when he says, "and gives to each one according to his deeds." The people respond, "According to Your mercy, O Lord, and not according to our sins."

From here, the Prayers of Institution begin, at the end of which the Holy Spirit descends upon the bread and transforms it into the Body, and the grape juice into the Blood of the Lord Jesus Christ. The prayer begins with Abouna taking the Lamb with his right hand and placing it on his left hand.

  • Pay attention: when Abouna takes the Lamb with his right hand, it symbolizes the Jews. Placing it on his left hand symbolizes the Gentiles, when the Jews handed the Lord Jesus Christ over to the Gentiles to be judged. Do you see the beautiful meaning? "For He will be delivered to the Gentiles and will be mocked and insulted and spit upon. They will scourge Him and kill Him. And the third day He will rise again." (Luke 18:32-33). And in the Book of Psalms, it says: "The kings of the earth set themselves, and the rulers take counsel together, against the Lord and against His Anointed." (Psalm 2:2).

Abouna begins to divide the Lamb from top to bottom, symbolizing the descent of the Lord Jesus Christ from His glory for the salvation of the world. He divides it without separating it completely. Then he divides a part above the Spadikon (Al-Isbadikon) and another part below the Spadikon. The upper part is called the Head, and the lower part is called the Feet, because in the prophecy (Genesis 49:10-11), Judah is the head and the tail. "I am the Alpha and the Omega, the Beginning and the End," as stated in the Book of Revelation.

  • The one-third on the right symbolizes the Son.
  • The two-thirds on the left symbolize the Father and the Holy Spirit.
  • (Another interpretation: The one-third represents the institution of the Mystery in the Upper Room. The two-thirds represent the completion of the Mystery on the Cross and the Resurrection).

This is the first stage of the Fraction. The Holy Spirit descends when the deacon calls out: "Worship God in fear and trembling." The whole church prostrates (Yasjud), and the Holy Spirit descends upon the oblations (Al-Qarabeen), transforming them into the true Body of the Lord Jesus Christ, and upon the juice of the vine in the Chalice, transforming it into the true Blood of Emmanuel our God.

The Introductory Prayer of Thanksgiving

Before the Fraction, Abouna places the two Veils (Lafa'if) that were on his hands onto the Altar. Then he takes the Pure Body—and here he says Body, because the Holy Spirit has descended upon the oblation and transformed it into a true Body. He begins an introductory Prayer of Thanksgiving:

"And again, let us give thanks to God the Pantocrator, the Father of our Lord, God, and Savior Jesus Christ..."

Please pay attention to this prayer and know why we are thanking God:

  1. "He has made us worthy now to stand in this holy place." Meaning, He received us in His house. Moreover, He is present with His Body and Blood on the Altar in His house or church. It is a generosity from God because He made us worthy to stand in His house, even though we are unworthy.
  2. "To lift up our hands." Meaning, to be in a state of prayer and brokenness throughout the Liturgy. Lifting the hands means prayer because we are in the presence of God. The period of the Fraction is a period of repentance, where you say, "O Lord, I thank You that You gave me a second chance for repentance and brokenness before You." The Fraction is the peak of love and sacrifice for our sake. It is a time for self-examination and confession before Him, because afterward, we will go in to partake of this Body and this Blood and unite with Him; we will enter into union with the Bridegroom.
  3. "And to serve His Holy Name." Standing in the presence of God, we serve Him as the angels serve Him in Heaven. We are earthly servants imitating the angels, so we should be in a state of continuous prayer, alert, because we are before the Body and Blood of Christ. And you, as a servant bearing His Holy Name—do you not say "I am Christian"? That means you bear His Name.
  4. "To make us worthy of the communion and partaking of His Holy Mysteries." At this moment, hold yourself accountable. You are standing in His house, He accepted you in His house, and you are lifting your hands to pray. Is there anything preventing you from Communion? Are you truly worthy to take the Body and Blood of the Lord Jesus Christ? Ask yourself!

In general, the people respond and say: "Amen." Meaning, we are worthy, and we believe in all that was asked and all that was said. "Amen" means confessing all that Abouna said.

Signing the Body with the Blood

Abouna completes the Prayers of Institution. He places his right index finger on the Body next to the Spadikon and says:

"The Holy Body."

The whole church prostrates and says: "We worship Your Holy Body."

He dips his right index finger into the Precious Blood, lifts it slightly, and makes one sign of the cross over the Blood inside the Chalice, saying:

"And the Precious Blood..."

The people respond: "And Your Precious Blood..."

(At this moment, he signs the Lamb with the Blood that is on his index finger. He starts from the side of the Spadikon on the left, goes around the back of the Body, returns above the Body from the right side of the Spadikon, goes up to the top of the Body, goes around the back of the Body, and comes up to the bottom side of the Spadikon. In this way, the Body is signed with the Blood in the sign of the Cross). [2]

Abouna continues and says: "Of His Christ, the Pantocrator, the Lord our God." The deacon says: "Amen, Amen, pray." And the people respond and say: "Lord have mercy." Because the Fraction represents the sufferings and torments of the Lord Jesus Christ.

Here, Christ gives peace a second time when Abouna says: "Peace be with all of you" without the sign of the Cross, because after this, any greeting of peace Abouna says is directly from the Lord Jesus Christ.

Some Important Information about the Fraction (Al-Qisma):

  • Signing the Body with the Blood: Indicates the union of His Divinity with His Humanity, and that this Blood is for this Body, and this Body is for this Blood.
  • When Abouna says, "And the Precious Blood," this represents the crucifixion of Christ, the shedding of His Blood, and the offering of Himself for the world. Therefore, the people cry out and say: "Lord have mercy."
  • The Fraction is the peak of love. Because the Lord Jesus Christ shows His love for the Church, who is the Bride, and reminds her that out of His love for her, He endured torments and sufferings, and shed His Blood on the Cross for her sake. During the Fraction, we must remember this love.
  • This division does not fall upon the actual Body of our Lord Jesus Christ, but upon the bread that represents His Body—upon the material used to complete the Sacrament. Just as water is used in Baptism and oil is used in the prayer of the Holy Unction. Here is the work of the Holy Spirit in the Sacraments of the Church. (Some accuse us, saying that we crucify and torture Christ in every Liturgy!)
  • The particle (Gawhara) that you receive in Communion is the complete Body of Christ, not a part of it. [3] Whether the size of the particle is small or large, it represents the complete Body of Christ, not a part of it. No power in existence can divide the Body of Christ; the division falls upon the "accidents" (Al-'Arad — the physical properties), not the essence (Al-Gawhar). "Not one of His bones shall be broken."
  • The sufferings of the Lord Jesus Christ did not touch His Divinity, because the Divinity is incapable of physical suffering. Saint Jerome clarified this when he said: "The Lord Jesus Christ patiently endured these sufferings for the salvific dispensation, but His body did not see corruption."
  • Likewise, they compared the Fraction to a word spoken by a person. Another person hears it, and if many people and countless numbers hear it, this word does not diminish; it reaches every one of them complete without being fragmented. Thus, the breaking of the bread, whether the piece is large or small, is the Bread.
  • Dividing the Body, as we will see in the Fraction, is into 12 parts, which is the number of the disciples of the Lord of Glory, and with them the Savior. A large cross in the center of the Oblation symbolizes the Lord Jesus Christ (the Spadikon), and around Him are His 12 disciples.
  • The Fraction has a spiritual meaning, which is the separation of the soul of the Lord Jesus Christ from His body, as happened on the Cross.
  • Dividing the Body signifies the sufferings that befell the Lord Jesus Christ, such as the scourging, carrying the Cross, being pierced with the spear, the crown of thorns, driving the nails into His pure body, and being struck on the face. The separations that Abouna makes between the particles are called "wounds." "But He was wounded for our transgressions, He was bruised for our iniquities; the chastisement for our peace was upon Him, and by His stripes we are healed." (Isaiah 53:5).

The Prayer of the Fraction consists of thanksgivings to God for this inexpressible gift. He gave us His Holy Body and Precious Blood so that we may live by them forever, and they help us in the days of our sojourning on earth—if we partake with repentance and worthiness. That is why at the end of the Fraction we say the Lord's Prayer, "Our Father who art in heaven," and the explanation of this will be in a separate lesson.

We will continue the Prayers of the Fraction next week. And to our God be all glory and honor forever. Amen.
