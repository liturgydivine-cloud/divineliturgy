The Commemoration of the Saints

Summary of the Previous Lesson:

The Seven Litanies (Al-Awashi / Supplications) are considered the Church's prayer for all creation and the entire inhabited world. The Church prays for all of these so that our Lord, the Lord of this whole universe, may encompass them with His mercy, give them heavenly blessings, and have compassion on all that His hands have made. Even in our private prayers, we must ask for God's mercies for ourselves, for others, and for all people—even for those who harbor evil against us.

What clarifies this is our teacher Paul the Apostle when he said:

"Therefore I exhort first of all that supplications, prayers, intercessions, and giving of thanks be made for all men, for kings and all who are in authority, that we may lead a quiet and peaceable life in all godliness and reverence." (1 Timothy 2:1-2).

The prayers of the Litanies have their immense value because they come after the descent of the Holy Spirit upon the Oblations (Al-Qarabeen). The Body and Blood of our Lord are now on the Altar (Al-Mazbah), which is considered one of the most awesome and holy times in the Divine Liturgy. Meaning, the Lord is present on the Altar. Abouna asks on behalf of all these as an intercessor for creation and creatures, just as it was before the fall. He asks as God's steward on earth, as our teacher Paul the Apostle said:

"Let a man so consider us, as servants of Christ and stewards of the mysteries of God." (1 Corinthians 4:1).

Who are the stewards of God? Of course, our Father the Patriarch, our Fathers the Metropolitans and Bishops, and our Fathers the Hegumens (Qamamisa) and Priests (Qusus). They are the stewards of the mysteries of God, who offer and pray the Seven Sacraments of the Church.

As I explained last time, the Seven Litanies are:

  1. The Litany of Peace: Abouna asks for peace for the One, Catholic, Apostolic Church, and to keep her in peace.
  2. The Litany of the Fathers: Abouna prays and asks for the Pope and all the Orthodox bishops, that the Lord our God may give them strength, grace, and wisdom in managing and shepherding the Church of God.
  3. The Litany of the Priests: A prayer for the priests and deacons—the seven ranks of the Church of God [1]—who assist the bishop in dividing (not just interpreting) the word of truth rightly and shepherding the people.
  4. The Litany of Mercy and the Servants: Abouna prays and asks for the servants, and asks for mercy for himself and for all the people.
  5. The Litany of the Place: Abouna prays for the salvation of the world, and the peace and tranquility of the world, because peace and tranquility reflect on the Church, her calm, and her peace.
  6. The Litany of the Waters, Crops, and Herbs: The Holy Synod combined the three Litanies into one Litany for the diaspora countries (like America), because they do not have the same agricultural seasons found in Egypt.
  7. The Litany of the Oblations: Abouna prays for all those who offered gifts to the Church to meet its needs, whether material or in-kind offerings like flour, candles, incense, oil, etc. Here, Abouna points to the Blood and Body with his hands when praying this Litany because they are considered the peak and symbol of all that the believers offer to the Church. (And before the Litany of the Oblations, Abouna prays a prayer deep in its meanings and words when he says: "Bring them up according to their measure... Give joy to the face of the earth... etc." I explained it in detail last time).

The Commemoration of the Saints (Al-Magma')

In the early Church, the Apostolic Church, there was no Magma' (Commemoration) because, in the beginning of its life, the Church did not have martyrs, saints, bishops, or monks in the sense they exist today. However, with the passing of years and centuries, the prayer of the Commemoration was added. It came through Ecumenical Councils and local synods, and the prayer of the Commemoration took many centuries to evolve and reach what it is today [2].

The Church continues to add certain saints who are recognized by the Holy Synod, such as Pope Kyrillos VI, Archdeacon Habib Girgis, Abouna Bishoy Kamel, the holy bishop Anba Sarapamon Abu Tarha, and the departed Anba Abraam, Bishop of Fayoum... etc.

The Theological Concept of the Prayer of the Commemoration:

The Commemoration, in truth, is a Litany (Oushia) or prayer offered by the Church for the sake of these saints who have been perfected in faith. That is, they lived their lives and presented their bodies as a living sacrifice to God until they finished the race during their time on earth and kept their orthodox faith.

In the Prayer of the Commemoration, the Church gives the concept and meaning of intercession (Shafa'a) and the cohesion of her departed members with her remaining members on earth—those who have been perfected in faith and those who have not yet been perfected and are still in a state of struggle (Gihad). We notice that the whole Church is around the Altar in the presence of the Lord Jesus Christ upon it. This means mutual prayers between the invisible Victorious Church and the visible Struggling Church, which is us.

The prayer of the Struggling Church on earth for the Victorious Church asks the Lord to increase their repose (Niyaha) and rest, and to forgive them any lukewarmness or negligence that happened while they were in the body [3]. They finished the race until the crown of righteousness was laid up for them. Our teacher Paul the Apostle prayed for one of his departed loved ones, Onesiphorus, saying: "The Lord grant to him that he may find mercy from the Lord in that Day." (2 Timothy 1:18).

Mentioning the Commemoration and the saints teaches us to emulate the biography of the saint and follow their footsteps in godliness. As Abouna says at the beginning of the Commemoration: "Those who have pleased You since the beginning." The prayer of the Commemoration, which includes the saints (members of the Victorious Church), means they are present with us in spirit.

Mentioning the holy fathers in the Commemoration is considered a command from the Only-Begotten Son of God, when He said that the saints should be remembered and what they did should be told wherever the Gospel is preached in the world:

"Assuredly, I say to you, wherever this gospel is preached in the whole world, what this woman has done will also be told as a memorial to her." (Matthew 26:13).

You know what happened in the house of Simon the Leper: "A woman came to Him having an alabaster flask of very costly fragrant oil, and she poured it on His head as He sat at the table." (Matthew 26:7).

Their names are spoken, and we remember their biographies, the very struggle they offered, and the virtue of each one of them. We see their love for the Lord Jesus Christ at its peak and as an example. We see that they gave themselves for the sake of God and the love of God, and they did not accept deliverance or spare their bodies, but endured everything out of their love for Christ the King. Each one offered his soul, his body, and his self as an oblation on the altar of the love of God in a different way. Every one has a love story and a special relationship with God; this in itself benefits us in our spiritual lives.

Praying the Magma'

Abouna begins the Commemoration (Al-Magma') as I said, praying and saying:

"For this, O Lord, is the command of Your Only-Begotten Son, that we share in the commemoration of Your saints."

He continues and says:

"Graciously accord, O Lord, to remember all the saints who have pleased You since the beginning."

He begins to mention who they are:

  • Our Holy Fathers, the Patriarchs: Who are they? Our father Abraham, our father Isaac, and our father Jacob; sometimes they are called our Fathers the Patriarchs.
  • The Prophets: From Moses first, then Joshua the son of Nun, until the last one, the Prophet Malachi. Each one had a mission for the salvation of the people of Israel and everyone who believes in his message. But the most beautiful thing is in the Liturgy of Saint Gregory, when Abouna prays in the Prayer of Reconciliation: "Neither an angel, nor an archangel, nor a patriarch, nor a prophet, did You entrust with our salvation; but You Yourself, without change, took flesh and became man, and resembled us in everything except sin alone. You became for us a Mediator with the Father..." He continues detailing the work of the Incarnate Hypostasis (Oqnum) of the Son for the salvation of the world.
  • The Apostles, Preachers, and Evangelists: Everyone who delivered the joyful Good News (Bishara) is considered an evangelist.

After several centuries, the Church began to grow and expand, and its fruits ripened. Who are they, O Lord? Abouna responds and prays, but before he mentions them, he knows there is something important that would be considered missing in the Commemoration, so he says:

"And the martyrs and confessors, and all the spirits of the righteous who were perfected in the faith." (These are on one scale).

On the other, important scale, he continues to pray and says:

"And most of all, the Saint full of glory, the ever-virgin Theotokos, the Holy and Pure Mary, who truly gave birth to God the Word."

The reason our Mother the Virgin precedes all those who came before:

  1. She is higher than all the heavenly beings.
  2. From her, God the Word took flesh. As we say in the Creed: "For us men and for our salvation He came down from heaven, and was incarnate of the Holy Spirit and of the Virgin Mary, and became man..." He took His Body or His Humanity from our Mother the Virgin, which is our body.
  3. Our Mother the Virgin is the intercessor before the throne of her Son on behalf of all the human race.

After our Mother the Virgin, he mentions the angel who prepared the way of salvation: John the Baptist. He is called the Forerunner (Al-Sabiq), meaning he preceded the Lord Jesus Christ to prepare the way of salvation, and the Baptizer (Al-Sabigh), referring to John's baptism of repentance with water. He offered his head for the Truth and the True God, and was slaughtered for the Truth.

Abouna mentions the first of the martyrs: Stephen, the Archdeacon. And of course, in the Egyptian Church, it is very important to mention the Preacher of the Egyptian Lands, the Beholder of God, the Evangelist Mark the Apostle, the Pure, who shed his blood in Egypt and in the streets of Alexandria.

After that, Abouna mentions the Father Patriarchs in the Church who defended the faith unto blood: like Saint Severus of Antioch and the Teacher of the Church, our Father Dioscorus, the Defender of the Faith. Our Father Athanasius the Apostolic, who truly struggled with the struggle of the Apostles against the Arian heresy and was exiled five times. Then Abouna mentions our Father the Patriarch and Seal of the Martyrs, the High Priest Peter, Pope number 17.

Abouna also mentions some Father Patriarchs from other churches because they offered services to the One, Catholic, Apostolic Church: like Saint Gregory the Theologian (Al-Natiq bil-Ilahiyat), and Saint John Chrysostom, who was exiled from his throne and slaughtered in his exile for the sake of truth and impartiality. And Saint Basil the Great, the author of the Liturgy we always pray with, and the rest of the saints in other churches who left marks on the history of the Church.

Abouna mentions in the prayer of the Commemoration the three Ecumenical Councils (Al-Magami' Al-Maskuniyya). They were all witnesses to our Lord and the faith they received, and they preserved the orthodox faith of the Apostolic Church [4].

After that, we find Abouna entering the life of monasticism in the prayer of the Commemoration. He mentions our holy monk fathers: the father and founder of monasticism, Anba Antony; Anba Paul, the first of the anchorites (Sawah); the three holy Macarii; Anba Bishoy, the beloved of Christ; Anba Pachomius, the father of communal monasticism (Koinonia), and Theodore his disciple; the head of the solitaries, Anba Shenouda, and Anba Wissa his disciple; and the strong Saint Anba Moses the Black (and Martyr), who gives us an example of wrestling against sin unto death.

Of course, every name in the Commemoration has its own image. Each is a beautiful, luminous icon and an example for us in spiritual struggle, so that we may follow their footsteps and learn from their pure biographies.

He concludes the prayer of the Commemoration and says:

"And all the choirs of Your saints. Through their prayers and supplications, have mercy on us all together, and save us for the sake of Your Holy Name which is called upon us."

The whole church responds with this prayer, saying:

"Glory to You, O Lord; O Lord, glory to You. Lord have mercy. Lord bless us. Lord repose them, Amen."

At the end of the Commemoration in the Liturgy of Saint Cyril, there are strong, deep words that express what our feelings should be when we remember these saints who offered beautiful icons in the life of the Church. Abouna prays and says:

"For we, O our Master, are not worthy to intercede for the blessedness of those; but rather they, standing before the judgment seat of Your Only-Begotten Son, intercede on our behalf for our poverty and weakness. Be forgiving of our iniquities for the sake of their holy supplications, and for the sake of Your blessed Name which is called upon us."

The Serving Deacon in the Sanctuary responds and says:

"Let the readers (Aghnostos) say the names of our holy fathers the Patriarchs... etc."

Who are the "readers"? They are the deacons in the rank of Reader (Aghnostos). They are supposed to recite the names of our Father Patriarchs from Mark the Apostle up to the current Pope—the Patriarchs who have fallen asleep in the faith since the beginning. However, the deacons usually chant the hymn Pi-Nishti (The Great Anba Antony) or the hymn Epshois (Through the prayers and intercessions of the All-Holy... etc.) instead of reading the names of the 118 Patriarchs [5].

This is the prayer of the Commemoration in the Divine Liturgy. We have seen how it evolved and how the number of saints mentioned in the Commemoration increased over the years and centuries. The additions come from Ecumenical Councils or local synods. And to our God be all glory and honor forever. Amen.
