The Praxis & Synaxarium

Review

Last time, I spoke with you about the readings in the Liturgy, specifically the General Epistles (the Catholicon / Al-Catholicon). As I informed you, the Father Priest does not leave the Sanctuary (Al-Haykal), and there is no incense or procession (Dora). This signifies the presence of the Apostles in Jerusalem and their not going out to preach. This was according to the command of the Lord of Glory to them not to depart from Jerusalem until the Holy Spirit descended upon them and they received power from on high. Indeed, they remained in Jerusalem until the day the Holy Spirit descended upon them.

Of course, you know what happened after the descent of the Holy Spirit on the disciples, how the New Testament Church was founded by the action and power of the Holy Spirit within them, and as you know, the sermon of Saint Peter the Apostle after which 3,000 souls believed in one day.

I also spoke to you about the books of readings, which are called the Katamaros (Katamaros):

  • Katamaros of the Days.
  • Katamaros of the Sundays.
  • Katamaros of Great Lent and Jonah's Fast.
  • Katamaros of the Holy Fifty Days.

Then I explained the kinship of Saint James and Jude, the sons of Alphaeus (or Clopas), to the Lord Jesus Christ. I also summarized for you the General Epistles and their purpose, which are:

  • The Epistle of our teacher James the Apostle, the brother of the Lord.
  • The First and Second Epistles of our teacher Peter the Apostle.
  • The Epistles of our teacher John the Beloved, which are three epistles.
  • The Epistle of our teacher Jude.

Out of the Church's care for those listening to the readings, the deacon always says at the end of the Catholicon:

"Do not love the world or the things in the world. The world is passing away, and the lust of it; but he who does the will of God abides forever. Amen." [1]

This reminds us of the passing away of the world and everything in it. What will remain are a person's deeds and their treasures that they stored and gathered in Heaven; their deeds go before them.

The Reading of the Praxis (Al-Praxis) and the Synaxarium (Al-Synaxar)

Today, I continue with you the Liturgy of the Word (Al-Quddas Al-Ta'limi), specifically the reading of the Praxis and the Synaxarium.

The word Praxis is a Greek word meaning "Acts." As you know, the one who wrote the Praxis—the Book of the Acts of the Apostles—is Saint Luke the Evangelist. He left it open without a conclusion [2], unlike the rest of the books, on the basis that:

  1. This book is the history of the Church, and it will increase as long as the Church is alive and present in the world.
  2. He knew by the Spirit that after the Apostles would come the successors of the Apostolic Fathers (their disciples), and after them the Patriarchs, Bishops, Martyrs, Confessors, and Saints. All of these would work in the Church, complete it, and create for it a history added to the Book of Acts—which is the Book of the Synaxarium. That is why it is read after the Praxis, as a completion to it.

From the beauty of the Church, she made the ordination (Siyama/Rasama) of new Patriarchs and Bishops take place after the reading of the Praxis and the Synaxarium. Why? Because she considers the ordination of a new Pope or Bishop a continuation of the work of the Apostles, as they are their successors, and a new page in the history of the Church and the extension of the Kingdom on earth (which is the Struggling Church).

The Praxis has its prayers and its procession (Dora) around the Altar. It begins after the reading of the Catholicon. While the deacons and the people are chanting the hymn, "Hail to you, Mary, the beautiful dove..." (Shere Ne Maria) [3]—which is the Praxis Response (Marad Al-Praxis)—this response varies according to the occasion in the Church.

For example, right now, while we are in Great Lent, the response chanted is:

"Remember me, O Lord; remember me, O my God; remember me, O my King, when You come into Your kingdom." [4]

The Prayers of the Praxis (Salawat Al-Praxis):

Abouna stands before the Altar (Al-Mazbah), and the Serving Deacon presents the Censer (Shorya) to him. As he prays, he says:

"Glory and honor, honor and glory to the Holy Trinity, the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit, now and at all times and unto the ages of all ages, Amen."

He places one hand [spoonful] of incense in the censer and prays inaudibly (Sirran), saying:

"O God, who accepted the burnt offering of Abraham, and instead of Isaac prepared for him a sheep. Even so, accept from us, O our Master, this burnt offering of incense, and send us in return Your abundant mercy. Make us to be pure from the stench of sin, and make us worthy to serve before Your goodness, O Lover of Mankind, in purity and righteousness all the days of our life." [5]

Then he continues and says: "We ask You, O our Master..." and begins to pray the Three Short Litanies while circling the Altar (just like the Pauline Procession), which are: the Litany of Peace, the Litany of the Fathers, and the Litany of the Assemblies. Then, Abouna exits the Sanctuary (Al-Haykal), performs the Incense Cross (Saleeb Al-Bukhur), enters once again to give a hand of incense to his brother Partner Priest, and goes out to perform the Praxis Procession (Doret Al-Praxis).

The Praxis Procession (Doret Al-Praxis)

This procession is different from the Pauline Procession because it is short and goes in the opposite direction. It starts from right to left. While praying the Incense of the People, he says:

"The blessing of my lords the Apostolic Fathers—our father Peter, our teacher Paul, and the rest of the disciples—may their holy blessing be with us, Amen."

Why is the procession short, and why does the Priest move from right to left (opposite to the Pauline Procession)? Because Saint Paul labored more abundantly than the rest of the Apostles. He preached, evangelized, traveled, and served in many countries more than the other Apostles, covering a wider sphere in evangelism. He himself said: "In labors more abundant, in stripes above measure..." (2 Corinthians 11:23) [6].

  • The Priest's exit from the Sanctuary to offer incense in the church before the choir of deacons symbolizes the Pure Apostles leaving Jerusalem to preach and teach.
  • The fact that the Priest does not circle the entire church indicates that the Apostles initially restricted their work to Judea and the cities of Judah and Samaria.
  • The Priest moving in the opposite direction of the Pauline Procession (from right to left) signifies the return of the Apostles from the Mount of Olives to Jerusalem after the Ascension of the Lord Jesus.

There is another interpretation: The Pure Disciples and Apostles wanted to preach to their Jewish brethren first—this is when he goes towards the right. But when they rejected them, they left Judea. When Abouna goes towards the left, it symbolizes that the Pure Apostles left Judea and went to the world and the Gentiles, just as Paul the Apostle traveled. Some went to India, North Africa, and Egypt, like Mark the Apostle.

The Mystery of the Praxis Return (Sirr Raj'at Al-Praxis):

Abouna returns and prays the Mystery of the Praxis Return, saying:

"O God, who accepted the confession of the thief on the honored Cross, accept unto You the confessions of Your people, and forgive them all their sins for the sake of Your Holy Name which is called upon us. According to Your mercy, O Lord, and not according to our sins."

At this moment, the Deacon is standing at the door of the Sanctuary (Al-Haykal) and takes the Censer (Shorya) from Abouna before he enters the Sanctuary.

Why? What is the reason? This action has a beautiful meaning and understanding it reveals a great mystery. Why does Abouna not enter the Sanctuary with the censer? Because our Pure Fathers the Apostles, after they left Jerusalem to preach in the world, did not return to it. Rather, each one was martyred in the country where he was evangelizing and preaching. They did not return again to Jerusalem [7]. Contemplate the beauty of the Liturgy!

An Important Note

With the three processions (Dora) Abouna made in the Praxis Procession, the total number of processions he has made becomes seven (7). They are:

  • Three (3) processions in the Pauline Epistle (Al-Bolis).
  • In the Mystery of the Pauline Return (Sirr Al-Raj'aa), he circles the Altar once. So, the total Pauline processions are four (4).
  • Plus three (3) processions in the Praxis (Al-Praxis).
  • The total is seven (7) processions.

What does this remind you of? These seven processions remind us of the Children of Israel when they marched around the walls of Jericho carrying the Ark of the Covenant seven times until the walls of Jericho fell (Joshua 6) [8].

Abouna's processions around the Altar (Al-Mazbah), which are full of prayers and outpouring before God, as I explained to you, must cause the walls of evil to fall, the strongholds of sin to be demolished, the fortresses of vices to crumble, and our hearts to be purified from every earthly thought during the Liturgy. Let us begin to enter into the depths of the prayers of the Liturgy up to Communion (Al-Tanawul).

The Synaxarium (Al-Synaxar)

It is a Greek word meaning "News" or "Compilations" that tells the history of the fathers, prophets, patriarchs, bishops, priests, martyrs, confessors, and saints. Not just in Egypt, but from all the churches of the inhabited world before the Council of Chalcedon, which divided the churches and tore apart the unity of the churches and the orthodox faith.

Why do we read these stories?

  1. To remember them as an example: As lights for us on the path of the Church's struggle. We remember their labors, their struggle, and the end of their lives, how they went to Christ whom they loved and gave themselves for out of love for Him, and their works follow them. There, the Lord will wipe away every tear from their eyes (Revelation 7:17).
  2. To know the biographies of these heroes and the history of the Church: Through reading the Synaxarium, we learn what men, women, children, martyrs, and confessors saw, suffered, and endured from pain, troubles, and persecutions to preserve the orthodox faith handed down by the Pure Apostolic Fathers. They handed it down to us and left us a sound, orthodox faith.
  3. To learn from the stories of the holy fathers' struggle: In asceticism, fasting, repentance, prostrations (Metanoias), and prayers for the Kingdom of Heaven. We also learn that a life of holiness is not easy and that the Kingdom of Heaven suffers violence, as the Lord of Glory said: "The kingdom of heaven suffers violence, and the violent take it by force." (Matthew 11:12).

The wondrous thing about the Synaxarium is that it celebrates the day of the saint's martyrdom or departure (Niyaha), and does not celebrate their birthdays. It always says: "On this day, the Church celebrates the feast of the martyrdom of so-and-so, or the departure of Saint so-and-so..." (whether Patriarch, Bishop, Priest, or Deacon). There are also stories and histories about laypeople who had no clerical ranks but were examples of holiness and self-sacrifice, and many of them are found in the pages of the Synaxarium.

Everything written in the Synaxarium is for contemplation and teaching. We understand that it is not about the beginning, but about a good end. The Book of Ecclesiastes says:

"The end of a thing is better than its beginning... the day of death is better than the day of one's birth." (Ecclesiastes 7:1, 8).

Why is it called a "Feast"? Because on this day, the angels escort the souls and spirits of these saints to the Heavenly Bridegroom with inexpressible and glorious joy. That is why the Psalm says:

"Precious in the sight of the Lord is the death of His saints." (Psalm 116:15)

and Solomon the Wise says:

"The memory of the righteous is blessed." (Proverbs 10:7).

That is why you will find the Church in continuous feasts. At the same time, she is telling the people: "Complete the journey—the journey of your struggle—and walk in the path of the holy fathers. Considering the outcome of their conduct, imitate their faith." (Hebrews 13:7).

The Synaxarium covers the 30 days of every Coptic month with stories, tales, and biographies of the saints.

  • In the first 15 days of each month, the Father Priest says: "May God make it begin in goodness."
  • In the second 15 days of the month (meaning from the 16th day), the Father Priest says: "May God make it end in goodness."

The Introduction to the Synaxarium: Abouna says:

"In the name of the Father, and the Son, and the Holy Spirit, One God, Amen. The [number] day of the blessed month of [Coptic Month name]. May God make it begin (or end) in goodness and renew it for us in peace and tranquility, while our sins and iniquities are forgiven through the tender mercies of our Lord, O my fathers and brothers, Amen."

Next time, I will complete the last part of the Liturgy of the Word, which is the reading of the Gospel. This is considered the crown of all the readings because the Speaker is the Lord Jesus Christ Himself. To our God be all glory and honor forever. Amen.
