The Prayer of the Psalms, and the Choosing and Signs of the Lamb

Review

The most beautiful thing in our lives is the Divine Liturgy (Al-Quddas Al-Ilahi). It is from it that we receive the forgiveness of sins, and from it we receive eternal life, because everyone who eats of this Body and drinks of this Blood has their sins forgiven.

"Given for the remission of sins and eternal life to those who partake of Him." [1]

The word "Liturgy" (Quddas), as we said, means that the Lord of Glory, the Holy of Holies, is present with us on the Altar or the Holy Table (Al-Ma'ida Al-Muqadasa). He is the One who sanctifies the Oblations (Al-Qarabeen). That is why it is called Quddas. And everyone who partakes of these Holies (Qudsat) is sanctified. Besides that, they are also sanctified by their prayers in the Divine Liturgy.

We also said that we pray the Liturgy, not attend the Liturgy. There is a difference between the two. We agreed that we will divide the Liturgy into ten points.

The First Part: The Liturgy of the Word (or Catechumens). We divided it into 3 parts:

  1. The Prayer of the Psalms (Agpeya).
  2. The Choosing (Ikhtiyar) and Signs (Rushumat) of the Lamb.
  3. The Readings (Al-Qira'at).

The Second Part: The Liturgy of the Faithful. We divided it into 7 parts:

The First Three Parts are:
4. The Prayer of Reconciliation (Salat Al-Solh).
5. "It is meet and right" (Mostahaq wa Adel).
6. "Agios, Agios" (Quddus, Quddus, Quddus - Holy, Holy, Holy).

The Second Four Parts are:
7. The Litanies (Al-Awashi).
8. The Commemoration of the Saints (Al-Magma').
9. The Fraction (Al-Qisma).
10. Communion (Al-Tanawul).

By the grace of God, we start today with the Prayer of the Psalms (Agpeya) and the Choosing and Signs of the Lamb.

The Prayer of the Psalms (Agpeya)

Our Priest (Abouna) and the deacons put on their white tunics (Tunia) to begin the service of the Liturgy with the Prayer of the Psalms. Abouna stands to the right of the Altar and begins by saying:

"Lord have mercy, Amen, Alleluia" (Epchois Nai Nan Amen Alleluia).
  • We pray the 3rd and 6th Hours on days without strict fasting (Sawm Inqita'i), like Saturday, Sunday, Monday, Tuesday, Thursday, and during the Holy Fifty Days (Al-Khamasin).
  • If there is strict fasting, like on Wednesday and Friday, we pray the 3rd, 6th, and 9th Hours.
  • Of course, during Jonah's Fast, Great Lent, and the Paramon Fast, we pray from the 3rd Hour until the Compline (Al-Nom).

The deacon begins distributing the Psalms among those present in the church. The Gospel of each hour is read, and Abouna prays the Litanies (Qita'). The litanies are distributed among the priests present and the deacons. At the end of the first litany, we say: "Glory be to the Father, and to the Son, and to the Holy Spirit." And in the second litany, we say: "Now and at all times and unto the ages of all ages, Amen."

Why are the Psalms read before choosing the Lamb? Because the Psalms contain many prophecies about the life of the Lord Jesus Christ. That is why we pray the Psalms and the Hours (Al-Sawa'i); because they prepare us and make us ready for the Choosing of the Lamb. It is a preparation and spiritual readiness for the Rite and the worshipers. It confirms the presence of the Lamb along with the flask of wine, because it tells the story of the Lord's coming, His incarnation, and His sufferings. The Psalms themselves speak about Christ who will be offered as a sacrifice.

Also, the Psalms represent our state in the Old Testament, and the offering of the Lamb represents the New Testament; meaning, it shows the sequence of salvation. (Note: If the Plate of the Lamb / Tabaq Al-Hamal is not present in the church, the Psalms of the Hours are not prayed until the Lamb is brought in).

  • The Prayer of the 3rd Hour: Reminds us of Pilate's judgment upon the Lord of Glory, and at the same time, reminds us of the descent of the Holy Spirit upon the pure Disciples. Meaning, it reminds us of the events that happened with the Lord Jesus Christ and the Holy Disciples.
  • The Prayer of the 6th Hour: Reminds us of the crucifixion of the Lord Jesus Christ, the driving of the nails into His hands and feet, and how He tasted bitterness and vinegar and did not want to drink. This was for the sake of each one of us and for our salvation.
  • The Prayer of the 9th Hour: Reminds us of the right-hand thief's request to be remembered and accepted into His Kingdom. This is hope for everyone, no matter their sins, if they are sincere in their repentance. It also reminds us of the death of the Lord Jesus Christ on the Cross, His giving up the spirit, and the beginning of the New Testament, the covenant of Grace. (The sun was darkened, nature mourned, the veil of the temple was torn in two).

Similar contemplations apply to the rest of the prayers of the Hours.

After the Prayer of the Psalms, the deacons chant "We Exalt You, O Mother of the True Light" (Nu'azzimuki), reminding us of our Mother the Virgin who bore for us the Savior of the world. After that, we say the Creed (Qanun Al-Iman). This is the Church's declaration of her faith in Christ: His birth, crucifixion, resurrection, and the expectation of His Second Coming. The Creed is recited by everyone present in the church audibly, meaning in a loud voice.

During this time, Abouna who will offer the Lamb washes his hands while reciting verses from the Psalms [2]:

"Purge me with hyssop, and I shall be clean; wash me, and I shall be whiter than snow." (Psalm 51:7)
"Make me hear joy and gladness, that the bones You have broken may rejoice." (Psalm 51:8)
"I will wash my hands in innocence; so I will go about Your altar, O Lord, that I may proclaim with the voice of thanksgiving." (Psalm 26:6-7)

This means the Liturgy begins with joy and ends with joy.

The Choosing (Ikhtiyar) and Signs (Rushumat) of the Lamb

Abouna takes a Veil (Lafa'if) from the Altar, stands facing the people, and says:

"O Lamb of God, who takes away the sins of the whole world, hear us and have mercy on us."

The deacons, and ideally the entire congregation, begin saying "Lord have mercy" (Kyrie Eleison) 41 times [3], representing the number of lashes the Lord Jesus Christ received, the nails, the crown of thorns, etc. The entire church asks for God's mercies with one voice, one heart, one spirit, and one mind.

During this time, Abouna stands in front of the Plate of the Lamb (Tabaq Al-Hamal or Tabaq Al-Qurban). The second priest stands on the other side. A deacon carries the flask of wine (Abaraka) in his right hand and a candle in his left hand. Another deacon carries the flask of water in his right hand and a candle in his left hand.

The Symbolism of the Candle: The Lord Jesus Christ came as the Light of the world. Also, the Prophet Isaiah said:

"The people who walked in darkness have seen a great light; those who dwelt in the land of the shadow of death, upon them a light has shined." (Isaiah 9:2).

It is held in the left hand because it follows the right hand carrying the wine (Abaraka), illuminating the path of the sacrifice. (Note: Abouna who chooses the Lamb, the priest standing opposite him holding the plate of oblations, and the two deacons on either side form the shape of a Cross).

Abouna places his hands in the shape of a Cross, reminding us of our father Jacob when he blessed the sons of Joseph, Manasseh and Ephraim [4]. The Church adopted this beautiful image in the Choosing of the Lamb.

The number of Oblations (Qurban) for the Lamb must be odd: 3, 5, 7, or 9. Abouna chooses the Lamb—an Oblation (Qurbana) without blemish. The Seals (Akhtam) must be clear, the writing clear, and without any cracks, because the Lamb of God, the Lord Jesus Christ, was without blemish.

Let us take an idea about the Oblation of the Lamb (Qurbanat Al-Hamal):

  • Round: Because the Lord Jesus Christ has no beginning and no end. He is the First and the Last, the Alpha and the Omega. The Lord Jesus Christ is the Sun of Righteousness [5], so it must be round.
  • No Salt or Sugar: Salt preserves things from corruption, but the Lord Jesus Christ manifested Himself in the flesh, so He does not need anything to preserve Him. No sugar, because sugar is a symbol of a life of luxury (Tana'um), and the Lord Jesus Christ came for suffering, not for luxury.
  • Yeast (Khamira): Yeast is placed in the dough of the oblation or the Lamb. Yeast symbolizes sin. The Lord Jesus Christ came to the world to bear the sins of the world and for the salvation of the world. That is why we put yeast. When the yeast enters the oven, it dies—a beautiful symbol of how the Lord Jesus Christ bore our sins on the wood of the Cross and died for us [6].
  • The Spadikon (Al-Isbadikon): In the middle of the oblation, there is a large cross called the Spadikon (Master/Lord) [7], symbolizing the Lord Jesus Christ. Around it are 12 crosses symbolizing the Pure Apostles, and five holes symbolizing the wounds of the Lord Jesus Christ.
  • Wheat (Hinta): The oblation is made from wheat flour, as the Scripture described us as wheat that was ground ("Bruised for our iniquities," "Unless a grain of wheat falls into the ground and dies"). Wheat symbolizes the children of God. We became flour, and the Lamb was made from this flour. We were gathered together to form the oblation of the Lamb. The Lord Jesus Christ is in the center, surrounded by the Apostles, and then we are around Him on the outside. This is a miniature symbol of Heaven.

Regarding the Abaraka (Grape Juice/Wine):

Similarly, as the Lord Jesus Christ said,

"I am the vine, you are the branches" (John 15:5).

We gathered all the grapes from the branches, pressed them, and they became the juice of the vine. We were all pressed together to remain in a miniature form in the flask of wine (Abaraka). The juice of the vine also symbolizes all nations, ten thousands times ten thousands, and thousands of thousands.

After Choosing the Lamb:

After Abouna chooses the Lamb that has no blemish, he begins to wipe it with the Veil (Lafa'if) he has, to clean off anything like flour or dust from the oblation of the Lamb. (Here, Abouna can remember the people who asked him to remember them).

Why does Abouna place the veil in the sleeve of his tunic (Tunia) before choosing the Lamb? This symbolizes the knife that our father Abraham carried and hid from his son Isaac before sacrificing him.

Then Abouna takes some wine (Abaraka) with his thumb from the flask and signs (Rashm) the Lamb and the rest of the oblations in the Basket (Sabt Al-Qurban). He kisses the rest of the oblations in the basket with the Lamb. He leaves the basket of oblations outside the Sanctuary (Al-Haykal), symbolizing the Pure Apostles who went out and preached throughout the whole inhabited world.

The Signs (Rushumat) of the Lamb:

Abouna enters the Sanctuary (Al-Haykal) with the Lamb, accompanied by the deacons carrying the flask of wine (Abaraka) and the flask of water. Abouna takes water from the flask and baptizes the Lamb [8].

(Take note: The Liturgy tells the story of Redemption / Al-Fidaa—from the incarnation and birth of the Lord Jesus Christ, His baptism by John the Baptist, His crucifixion, His resurrection, and His ascension to the heavens).

During the baptism of the Lamb, Abouna remembers everyone who asked him to remember them, and the papers presented on the Altar. This is important: anyone who has a request should give it to Abouna before the baptism of the Lamb!

Abouna prays to God to make it an acceptable sacrifice before Him for the sins and ignorances of His people. He prays for the peace of the Church, and he prays for the Father Priest serving with him and all the servants. Abouna wraps the Lamb in the Veil (Lafa'if) and places it on the Cross inverted [9], just as the Lord Jesus Christ carried the Cross and went to Golgotha. Wrapping the Lamb in the veil symbolizes the swaddling clothes placed on the Child Jesus, and it also symbolizes the Burial Shrouds (Al-Akfan).

There are 3 important things Abouna says at the Sanctuary (Al-Haykal) door while carrying the Lamb:

  1. "Glory and honor, honor and glory to the All-Holy Trinity, the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit."
  2. "Peace and edification to the One, Only, Holy, Catholic, and Apostolic Church of God."
  3. "Remember, O Lord, those who have brought unto You these gifts (Al-Qarabeen), those on whose behalf they have been brought, and those by whom they have been brought. Give them all the heavenly reward."

The Serving Deacon Responds: "Pray for these holy and precious gifts (Al-Qarabeen), our sacrifices, and those who have brought them. Lord have mercy."

The Priest circles around the Altar with the deacons, like Simeon the Elder when he carried the Lord Jesus Christ [10]. Abouna returns and stands at the Altar and begins the Signs (Rushumat) of the Lamb. He places the Lamb on his left hand and holds the Cross in his right hand. He begins and says:

  • The First Sign: "In the name of the Father, and the Son, and the Holy Spirit, One God, Amen. Blessed be God the Father the Pantocrator."
  • The Second Sign: "Blessed be His Only-Begotten Son, Jesus Christ our Lord."
  • The Third Sign: "Blessed be the Holy Spirit the Comforter."

The Deacon has this beautiful response for the Holy Trinity when he says:

"One is the Holy Father. One is the Holy Son. One is the Holy Spirit. Amen. Blessed be the Lord God forever, Amen. Praise the Lord, all you nations! Laud Him, all you peoples! For His merciful kindness is great toward us, and the truth of the Lord endures forever. Amen. Alleluia." [11]

The Congregation and Deacons outside continue glorifying the Holy Trinity and say: "Glory be to the Father, and to the Son, and to the Holy Spirit, now and at all times and unto the ages of all ages." With this, the Signs (Rushumat) of the Lamb are finished. Abouna places it in the Paten (Siniya), symbolizing the Manger or the Tomb.

The Prayer of Thanksgiving starts here

After the Signs of the Lamb, Abouna takes the flask of wine (Abaraka) from the deacon and begins the Prayer of Thanksgiving. As Abouna prays, he begins to empty the flask of wine into the Chalice (Kass). After emptying it, he adds one-third of the flask's volume of water and mixes it with the wine in the Chalice. This symbolizes the Blood and Water that came out from the side of the Lord Jesus Christ [12].

Abouna says the Prayer of Thanksgiving: "Let us give thanks to the Beneficent and Merciful God... for He has covered us, helped us, guarded us..."

The Deacon responds: "Pray that God may have mercy and compassion on us, hear us, help us, and accept the supplications and prayers of His saints for that which is good (Salah) on our behalf at all times..."

Abouna continues the Prayer of Thanksgiving until he reaches:

"All envy, all temptation, all the work of Satan, the counsel of wicked men, and the rising up of enemies, hidden and manifest, take them away from us..." (Here he signs himself). "...and from all Your people..." (He signs the people). "...and from this your holy priestly table, and from this holy place of Yours." (The third sign is on the Altar/Sanctuary).

The Prayer of Offering the Bread and the Chalice (Salat Taqdimat Al-Khubz wal-Kass)

Before placing the Prospharin (Al-Prospharin), Abouna prays a secret prayer inaudibly (Sirran):

"O Master, Lord Jesus Christ, the Co-Eternal Word... You are the Living Bread which came down from heaven... Show Your face upon this bread... and upon this cup..."

Here, he makes three signs over the bread and the chalice: Bless them, Sanctify them, Purify them and change them.

"That this bread may become Your Holy Body... And the mixture in this cup may become Your Precious Blood... That they may be to us all for participation and healing and salvation for our souls, bodies, and spirits..."

At the end of the prayer, the Priests spread the Prospharin (Al-Prospharin), symbolizing the stone placed at the door of the tomb [13]. Abouna folds the Veil (Lafa'if) into a triangle shape and places it on top, symbolizing the seals on the stone.

Everyone prostrates. The Partner Priest says the Absolution (Tahlil Al-Khudam). Then Abouna prays the Absolution of the Servants, mentioning the All-Holy Trinity, the Twelve Apostles, Mark the Apostle, Patriarch Saint Severus, Dioscorus, Athanasius the Apostolic, Peter the Seal of Martyrs, John Chrysostom, Cyril, Basil, and Gregory; and from the mouth of our honored Father, Pope Anba Tawadros II, and his partner Metropolitan Anba Serapion. Amen.

Footnotes

[1] Remission of sins: Echoing Matthew 26:28, quoted in the final Confession of the Liturgy. ↩
[2] Psalm Numbering: Using NKJV numbering; Coptic/Septuagint uses Pss 50 and 25. ↩

[7] Spadikon: From Greek Despotes (Master), the central part of the Qurban. ↩
[8] Baptizing the Lamb: Ritual washing symbolizing Christ's baptism in the Jordan. ↩
[9] Inverted Cross: Signifies humility and Christ bearing the Cross on His back. ↩
[10] Simeon the Elder: Symbolizes the presentation of Christ in the Temple (Luke 2:29). ↩
[11] Psalm 117: Calling all nations to praise God. ↩
[12] Water and Blood: Referring to the sacraments flowing from Christ's side (John 19:34). ↩
[13] Prospharin: Symbolizes the stone of the tomb; its shaking symbolizes the Resurrection earthquake. ↩
