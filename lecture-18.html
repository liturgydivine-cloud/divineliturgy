The Signs Before the Confession

Summary of the Previous Lesson:

I spoke with you about the Lord's Prayer ("Our Father") after the Fraction (Al-Qisma), and the secret prayers (Salawat Sirriyya) of the Father Priest during the responses of the deacons outside the Sanctuary (Al-Haykal) and the Serving Deacon inside the Sanctuary.

The Importance of the Lord's Prayer after the Fraction: It reveals our weaknesses as humans and is a warning against not forgiving the trespasses of others. We must be reconciled with ourselves and with others, and not carry in our souls any hatred or grudge against any person.

The One who taught us the Lord's Prayer is present on the Altar (Al-Mazbah). He gave us sonship, and we became His brothers. That is why when we say "Our Father," we are truly speaking to our Heavenly Father; we are truly talking to our Lord in Heaven. It is an opportunity to say it from our hearts.

The Lord's Prayer demonstrates our obedience to the teachings and commandments of the Holy Bible. Furthermore, it contains everything you need from our Lord in faith, because He listens to everyone in the church when we pray this prayer with depth and faith. (Generally, the "Our Father" prayer needs a lesson of its own).

When we say, "Give us this day our bread of tomorrow" [1]: The Eucharist (Eucharistia) is the Bread of the life to come, but we partake of it in our present life, live by it, and are nourished by it day by day until we reach our inheritance in eternity and the eternal life prepared for us.

"Give us this day our daily bread": This means this Bread (Communion) is our sustenance, sufficient for us, helping us against the snares of the enemy until we reach eternal life. The Eucharist is not of this age, but it is the earnest (Arboun / down payment) of eternal life. Because when we partake of Communion, the Lord Jesus Christ abides in us and we abide in Him. It is the earnest of eternal life in Heaven and our nourishment in this world until we reach the bosom of the saints.

The Lord's Prayer is very important after the Fraction, and as I said, we must implement the teachings of God found in this prayer.

At the end of the Lord's Prayer, we learned why the deacons outside the Sanctuary say "In Christ Jesus our Lord" in a long tune (Lahn), and the deacon inside responds in a long tune: "Bow your heads to the Lord." This is to give the Father Priest the opportunity to finish three secret prayers: 1. The Prayer of the Father (Salat Al-Aab). 2. The Prayer of Submission to the Father (Salat Al-Khudou' lil-Aab). 3. The Absolution to the Father (Tahlil Al-Aab).

And during these three prayers, you will also notice the deacons outside the Sanctuary saying, "Before You, O Lord, we bow and worship," in a long tune, and the Serving Deacon responds to them in a long tune: "Let us attend in the fear of God." This is so Abouna can complete the secret prayers quietly, deeply, and spiritually. I explained these three prayers in detail.

After these secret prayers, Abouna prays the Three Short Litanies (Al-Awashi), mentioning only the introduction of each Litany: the Litany of Peace, the Litany of the Fathers, and the Litany of the Assemblies. He says the latter audibly (Gahran) and says: "Remember, O Lord, our assemblies; bless them."

  • The deacon responds: "You have truly saved [us]" (Khallasta Haqqan). Here, he is addressing the Lord Jesus Christ who accomplished salvation on the Cross.
  • The second part is for the Father Serving Priest when he says: "And with your spirit."
  • And to the people when he says: "Let us attend in the fear of God."

Because the coming moments in the Liturgy are the moments of opening Paradise before us, which is Communion (Al-Tanawul). Just as the Lord Jesus Christ opened Paradise (Heaven) through the Cross. That is why the people must stand in reverence and cry out, saying "Lord have mercy, Lord have mercy, Lord have mercy," three times, asking for mercy and forgiveness.

The Signs Before the Confession (Rushumat ma qabl Al-I'tiraf)

Today, by the grace of God, we will take the penultimate part of the Liturgy, which is the Signs before the Confession.

After the people say, "Amen. Lord have mercy, Lord have mercy, Lord have mercy," the priest lifts the Spadikon (Al-Isbadikon) high above the Chalice (Kass) while bowing his head and says:

"The Holies for the Holy" (Ta agia tis agiois).

Lifting the Spadikon in this manner shows that this Mystery is high and elevated above human comprehension, and its holiness surpasses the mind and thought. We must be humble and stand in brokenness so that we may be sanctified.

Abouna lifts the Spadikon high above the Chalice, above the level of his head. Then he lowers it into the Chalice and dips its edge into the Blood [2]. After making the sign of the cross with the Spadikon, he lifts it again above the Chalice with his left hand under the Spadikon. He then takes it very carefully and begins to sign the Body with the sign of the Cross, signing the wounds of the Body with the Spadikon dipped in the Blood, while saying:

"Blessed is the Lord Jesus Christ the Son of God, and the sanctification of the Holy Spirit. Amen."

The spiritual meaning of this movement: The union of the Body with the Blood means that the Divinity never parted from His Humanity. Signing the Body with the Blood means that life united with the Body once again, which is what happened at the time of the Resurrection.

Abouna signs the Body three times with the Spadikon and the Blood in the prayers he will say next. This signifies the presence of the Body of the Lord Jesus Christ in the tomb for three days. And when he lifts the Spadikon again above the Chalice before placing it inside the Chalice, this is a symbol of the Resurrection after three days.

The moment Abouna says "The Holies for the Holy" is considered a warning from Abouna to everyone to hold themselves accountable. But God's mercies are vast, and we are truly sanctified by the prayers of the Liturgy.

When Abouna says, while signing the Body: "Blessed is the Lord Jesus Christ the Son of God, and the sanctification of the Holy Spirit. Amen," the people respond, and their response clarifies what Abouna said. The people say:

"One is the Holy Father, One is the Holy Son, One is the Holy Spirit. Amen."

The meaning of this response, as Saint John Chrysostom explained regarding "The Holies for the Holy," is that the people reply, saying: "God forbid, we are not holy; rather, we are sinners and unworthy of this title. Because the only Holy One, indeed the Holy of Holies, is Jesus Christ who incarnated for our salvation." That is why they respond and say: "One is the Holy Father... etc." [3]

The Liturgy is an invitation from God to be holy as the Father is holy, and to be perfect as the Father in Heaven is perfect. The concept of a "saint" (Qiddis) is someone who lives in the fear and love of God, keeps His commandments, and lives in continuous repentance (which is why we have the Mystery of Repentance and Confession in the Church). A saint does not necessarily have to perform miracles; that is a misconception.

The conclusion of the prayers from "The Holies for the Holy" to "One is the Holy Father": In the end, Abouna says a word that reassures the heart of every person that they are truly sanctified when he says: "Peace be with all of you." This peace is from the Lord of Glory on the lips of Abouna to reassure your hearts, while at the same time, we truly feel that we are unworthy or undeserving.

The Prayers of Confession (Salawat Al-I'tiraf)

During these prayers, Abouna signs the Body with the Spadikon dipped in the Blood and says:

"A Holy Body, and a true, precious Blood of Jesus Christ, the Son of our God. Amen."
"Holy and precious are the true Body and Blood of Jesus Christ, the Son of our God. Amen."

After this confession and this prayer, Abouna lifts the Spadikon, inverted, and places it in the Chalice (Kass), saying:

"The Body and Blood of Emmanuel our God, this is in truth. Amen."

Every time in these prayers of confession, which demonstrate that it is the true Body and true Blood of our Savior Jesus Christ, the people respond and say: "Amen."

Why did Abouna place the Spadikon inverted in the Chalice?

  • The Passover lamb is placed inverted on its back before it is slaughtered. It is tied to a roasting spit in the shape of a cross, with its legs facing upward. This is a symbol of the crucifixion of the Lord Jesus Christ, our Passover Lamb.
  • The Lord Jesus Christ was placed on His back on the Cross so that He could be nailed with nails in His hands and feet.
  • This indicates what Jacob the Patriarch prophesied about the Lord Jesus Christ, the Lion coming out of the tribe of Judah, saying: "He washed his garments in wine, and his clothes in the blood of grapes." (Genesis 49:11).

The Confession of the Priest before Communion (Al-I'tiraf)

The Father Priest covers the Paten (Siniya) with the Veil (Lafa'if) after placing the Dome (Qubba / Star) over it, and covers the Chalice as well. This means that the mysteries of the Church are unsearchable to any human being; it is a deep divine mystery that surpasses the mind.

The Priest lifts the Paten and begins to say the Confession (Al-I'tiraf). It is called a confession because the priest and all the people confess before approaching this pure sacrifice and these awesome mysteries, before receiving them into our hearts first, and then within ourselves.

The Text of the Confession: Abouna says in the text of the Confession:

"Amen. Amen. Amen. I believe, I believe, I believe and confess to the last breath, that this is the life-giving Body that Your Only-Begotten Son, our Lord, God, and Savior Jesus Christ, took from our Lady, the Queen of us all, the Holy Theotokos, the Pure Saint Mary. He made it one with His divinity without mingling, without confusion, and without alteration. He witnessed the good confession before Pontius Pilate. He gave it up for us upon the holy wood of the Cross, of His own will, for us all. Truly I believe that His divinity parted not from His humanity for a single moment nor a twinkling of an eye. Given for us for salvation, remission of sins, and eternal life to those who partake of Him. I believe, I believe, I believe that this is true. Amen." [4]

The truths found and included in this Confession include:

  1. What is placed before us on the Altar is in truth the Body and Blood of our Lord Jesus Christ.
  2. The Lord Jesus Christ made this Body one with His divinity without mingling, without confusion, and without alteration. (This is a response to the heresy of Nestorius).
  3. The Lord Jesus Christ suffered greatly by offering this Body on the wood of the Cross as an atonement for our sins, bearing all our transgressions.
  4. When we eat of this Body and drink of this Blood, we receive the forgiveness of our sins, and we obtain complete and perfect salvation from Him, and from the slavery of the devil. "He who eats My flesh and drinks My blood abides in Me, and I in him." (John 6:54-56).

The Deacon's Response & The Prayers Before Communion

After Abouna's Confession (Al-I'tiraf), the Serving Deacon begins this prayer immediately and says this response:

"Amen, Amen, Amen. I believe, I believe, I believe that this is so in truth. Amen. Pray for us, and for all the Christians who bade us to remember them in the house of the Lord. The peace and love of Jesus Christ be with you. Sing in the spirit, saying: Alleluia. Pray for the worthy partaking of these holy, pure, and heavenly mysteries. Lord have mercy." [5]

There are important prayers that Reverend Abouna says during the deacon's response, called the Prayers before Communion (Salawat ma qabl Al-Tanawul). They are four prayers:

  • The First Prayer:
    "Make us worthy, O our Master, to partake of Your Holy Body and Your Precious Blood, for the purification of our souls, bodies, and spirits, and for the remission of our sins and iniquities; that we may become one body and one spirit with You. Glory be to You, with Your Good Father and the Holy Spirit, forever. Amen."
  • The Second Prayer:
    "Loose, remit, and forgive us, O God, our iniquities which we have committed willingly and which we have committed unwillingly, which we have committed knowingly and which we have committed unknowingly, the hidden and the manifest, O Lord, forgive us, for the sake of Your Holy Name which is called upon us. According to Your mercy, O Lord, and not according to our sins." [6]
  • The Third Prayer:
    "O Prince of Life and King of the ages, Word of God the Father, our Lord and Savior Jesus Christ, the true Bread that came down from heaven, the Giver of Life to those who partake of Him: make us worthy, without falling into condemnation, to partake of Your Holy Body and Your Precious Blood. And let our partaking of Your Holy Mysteries make us one with You unto the end, and bless us. You are the Son of God. To You is the glory, with Him and the Holy Spirit, the Giver of Life, forever. Amen."
  • The Fourth Prayer:
    "O our Lord Jesus Christ, make us worthy to partake of Your Pure Body and Your Precious Blood, and let not our communion be unto condemnation. But as You said to Your pure disciples, 'Take of My Body and My Blood for the remission of your sins,' so also may we have fellowship with Your pure disciples by our partaking of Your life-giving Mysteries. That we may receive the remission of our sins, the pardon of our iniquities, purity for our souls, bodies, and spirits, and firmness in the orthodox faith in Your Holy Name unto our last breath, through the intercessions of the Pure Lady, Saint Mary, and all Your saints. Amen."

After these prayers, Abouna prostrates before the Altar, prays, and remembers whomever he wishes. The people and the deacons outside the Sanctuary respond and say: "Glory to You, O Lord, glory to You." Then he begins the distribution of the Mysteries (Al-Asrar). During the distribution of the Mysteries and Communion, the people chant Psalm 150.

And to our God be all glory and honor forever. Amen.

Footnotes

[1] Bread of Tomorrow: From the Greek epiousion, referring to the super-substantial Bread of the Kingdom given to us today in the Eucharist. ↩
[2] Dipping the Spadikon: The central part of the bread is dipped into the wine. This liturgical act portrays the reunion of Christ's Body and Soul at the Resurrection. ↩
[3] One is Holy: This exchange shows that the priest calls the people "Holy" because they have been sanctified, but the people humbly confess that only the Trinity is inherently Holy. ↩
[4] The Confession (Al-I'tiraf): The final declaration of orthodox Christology, affirming the real presence and the Hypostatic Union. ↩
[5] The Deacon's Confession: The deacon calls the congregation to respond practically by believing and approaching the sacrament worthily. ↩
[6] Prayer of Absolution: A hallmark of Coptic repentance, ensuring no sin is left unconfessed before God's mercy prior to receiving the Eucharist. ↩
