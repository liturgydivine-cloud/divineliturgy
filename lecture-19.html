Communion

Summary of the Previous Lesson:

We covered last time the signs (Rushumat) before the Confession (Al-I'tiraf), which is when the priest lifts the Spadikon (Al-Isbadikon) above the Chalice (Kass), bows his head, and says: "The Holies for the Holy." I explained why Reverend Abouna lifts his hand high above the Chalice: because this Mystery is high above human comprehension, and its holiness surpasses the mind and thought. In these moments, we must be humble and stand in brokenness so that we may be sanctified.

After that, Reverend Abouna places the Spadikon in the Chalice after making the sign of the cross. He dips its edge in the Blood and lifts it very carefully, placing his left hand underneath so that nothing of the Blood or the particles (Gawahir) of the Spadikon falls, while saying:

"Blessed is the Lord Jesus Christ the Son of God, and the sanctification of the Holy Spirit. Amen."

I explained the spiritual meaning of this movement in the prayers of the Liturgy; please refer back to the previous lesson.

When Reverend Abouna says: "Blessed is Jesus Christ the Son of God, and the sanctification of the Holy Spirit. Amen," as I said last time, the people respond: "One is the Holy Father, One is the Holy Son, One is the Holy Spirit. Amen." I explained to you why we say this response, which Saint John Chrysostom clarified for us: because while we are indeed sanctified by the prayers of the Liturgy, in reality, we are not holy by our own nature. That is why we say, "One is the Holy Father, One is the Holy Son."

We also covered the concept of a saint (Qiddis). It is one who lives in the fear of God and the love of God, keeps His commandments, and lives in continuous repentance (which is why we have the Mystery of Repentance and Confession in the Church). A saint is not defined as someone who performs miracles.

The Prayers of Confession: Abouna says them while signing the Body with the Spadikon dipped in the Blood, saying:

"A Holy Body, and a true, precious Blood of Jesus Christ, the Son of our God. Amen."
"Holy and precious are the true Body and Blood of Jesus Christ, the Son of our God. Amen."

After this confession and this prayer, Abouna lifts the Spadikon, inverted, and places it in the Chalice, saying: "The Body and Blood of Emmanuel our God, this is in truth. Amen." We learned why he said "Emmanuel" in the final sign, and I explained why Reverend Abouna places the Spadikon inverted in the Chalice.

The Confession of the Priest before Communion: We learned why Abouna places the Dome (Qubba) over the Paten (Siniya) and covers it with a veil (Lafa'if), as well as covering the Chalice. It means that the mysteries of the Church are unsearchable to any human being; it is a deep divine mystery that surpasses human thought.

I explained the Confession with references from the Gospel; please refer to the previous lesson. I will just remind you of the truths found in this Confession:

  1. What is placed before us on the Altar is in truth the Body and Blood of our Lord Jesus Christ.
  2. The Lord Jesus Christ made this Body one with His divinity without mingling, without confusion, and without alteration. He took it from the Lady of us all, the Pure Saint, the Virgin Mary.
  3. The Lord Jesus Christ suffered greatly by offering this Body on the wood of the Cross as an atonement for our sins, bearing all our transgressions. It also proves the historical period in which the crucifixion took place, which is the days of Pontius Pilate.
  4. When we partake of this Body and drink of this Blood, we receive the forgiveness of our sins and obtain complete and perfect salvation from Him, and we are no longer in the slavery of the devil, the enemy of good. "He who eats My flesh and drinks My blood abides in Me, and I in him."

After Abouna's Confession, the Serving Deacon says his specific response while holding the Cross in his right hand and a candle in his left hand, along with a veil folded in the shape of a triangle. The most important thing is that the deacon responds on behalf of the people to the Confession said by Reverend Abouna. The deacon asks and alerts the people to pray for Communion and for the communicants, because it helps make us worthy to partake of these Holy Mysteries. When he prays this response, all communicants are supposed to lift their hearts to God, asking for a blessing for themselves and all communicants, that it may be a blessing in their lives, establishing them in Christ Jesus and establishing Christ in them, and that their hearts may be pure without any grudge.

During the deacon's response, the priest prays four prayers called the Prayers before Communion. I explained these last time; refer back to the previous lesson.

The Meaning of the Cross, the Candle, and the Triangular Veil

Today, by the grace of God, we will take the meaning of the Cross, the candle, and the triangular Veil (Lafa'if) held by the Serving Deacon while facing the West before Abouna.

We notice that the Serving Deacon holds a veil shaped like a triangle, with a cross in his right hand and a candle in his left hand. As we know, when our father Adam and Eve sinned, they were cast out of Paradise so they would not eat from the Tree of Life, lest sin cling to them for their whole lives without salvation. So God placed the Cherubim at the gate of Paradise with a flaming, turning sword—which the candle symbolizes [1].

The Cross in the right hand is the symbol of salvation, when the Lord of Glory was crucified and bore all our sins. Through the Cross, He opened Paradise once again before Adam and all his descendants. The deacon says the response facing the West. At the end of the response, he stands behind Abouna facing the East, declaring that Paradise is now open for the communicants to eat from the Tree of Life, which is the Body and Blood of the Lord Jesus Christ.

When Abouna finishes the prayers before the Confession (Al-I'tiraf), he prostrates (Yasjud) before the Altar (Al-Mazbah) while saying inaudibly:

"That all glory, honor, and worship are due to the Holy Trinity, the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit, at all times."

The people and the deacons respond, saying: "Glory to You, O Lord; glory to You."

Communion (Al-Tanawul)

Abouna begins administering the Body. He first takes his own portion, and gives the Partner Priest his portion on the Spoon (Masteer / Mali'qah). After that, the Serving Deacon holding the triangular veil, Cross, and candle partakes with him, followed by the rest of the deacons.

Then he covers the Paten (Siniya) and carries it toward the people while saying: "The Holies for the Holy."

  • The meaning of displaying and presenting the Paten to the people is: it represents the appearance of the Savior to His disciples after the Resurrection, granting them the blessing with which He blessed them on the Mount of Olives before the Ascension.
  • As for displaying it a second time and then hiding it when he turns toward the Altar inside the Sanctuary (Al-Haykal), that signifies His Ascension before the disciples while they stood on the Mount of Olives, and His disappearance from them when a cloud took Him out of their sight [2].
  • There is a second interpretation: When the priest turns with the Paten to the people and the people cry out, "Blessed is He who comes in the name of the Lord," it means the First Coming of the Lord, who came for our salvation. The second time signifies His Second Coming to judge the inhabited world in righteousness. This fulfills the words of the Lord Jesus Christ when He said: "For I say to you, you shall see Me no more till you say, 'Blessed is He who comes in the name of the Lord!'" (Matthew 23:39).

Then Abouna begins to administer the Body to the people, and with him, the Partner Priest carries the Chalice (Kass) to administer the Blood of Emmanuel our God. The men partake first.

  • When administering the Body, Abouna says: "The Body of Emmanuel our God, this is in truth. Amen." The communicant responds and says: "Amen."
  • The same applies when administering the Blood; Abouna says: "The Blood of Emmanuel our God, this is in truth. Amen." The communicant responds and says: "Amen."

Then the women are administered in the same manner. After he finishes administering to the people, he returns with the Paten to the Altar. He, the Partner Priest, and the Serving Deacons in the Sanctuary consume what is left of the Body. Afterward, everyone consumes from the Blood. Then the Chalice is given to the Serving Priest to drink what remains in the Chalice. The priest and the Serving Deacon ensure that there are no small particles (Gawahir) left in the Paten. Then the priest begins—with the Serving Deacon assisting him—to wash the vessels (Al-Awani) after making certain no small particles remain.

Notes on Communion (Al-Tanawul):

  1. When we partake of the Holy Mysteries, we must have the feeling that we are in need of these Holy Mysteries, not that we are worthy of them.
  2. The communicant should not say to the Father Priest, "I sinned," or "Forgive me." This is incorrect, because Christ is before him with His Body. The person can ask about something they did before Communion, and it is up to what the Father Priest sees fit regarding whether they should commune or not [3].
  3. The one who partakes of the Body and Blood should not walk backward, but move forward. Because you have received the Body and Blood of the Lord Jesus Christ, do not go backward; the Lord Jesus Christ always pushes us forward, and whoever puts his hand to the plow does not look back.
  4. Communion of the Holy Mysteries in the Divine Liturgy and our union with the Lord Jesus Christ is an earnest (Arboun / down payment) of our eternal inheritance of eternal life, as well as a memorial and longing for His Second Coming to be with Him at all times.
  5. The moments of Communion of the Divine Mysteries are moments of eternity, light, and illumination for those dwelling on earth. Likewise, Communion is our heavenly food, the living Bread that came down from Heaven.
  6. Can you receive the Eucharist without the Church? Of course not, because the Church is the laboratory of the Holy Mysteries.
  7. Communion fulfills what the Savior said: "That you may eat and drink at My table in My kingdom." (Luke 22:30). The Altar is the Holy Table and the Kingdom of God, and the Church is a miniature picture of Heaven during the Divine Liturgy.
  8. Some people attend the Liturgy but do not take Communion, believing they are unworthy of Communion and being careful about the sanctity of the Mysteries. Of course, this is incorrect and a mistake. They deprive themselves of being bound to the Body of the Church, meaning they separate themselves from the Church. I urge those who have this thought to discuss it with the Father Priest or their Father of Confession (Ab I'tiraf)—because the Body of the Lord Jesus Christ is true food, and His Blood is true drink.
  9. Saint John Cassian says: "We must not avoid Communion under the pretext of feeling that we are sinners. We must partake often for the healing of the soul and the purification of the spirit, but with humility and faith, considering ourselves unworthy." [4]

Chanting Psalm 150 during Communion (Al-Tanawul)

It is an ancient Coptic tradition referred to by the scholar Clement of Alexandria [5], who was the head of the School of Alexandria from 150 to 200 AD. He says about this Psalm:

  • "Praise Him with the sound of the trumpet": Because with the sound of the trumpet, He will call the departed to the Resurrection.
  • "Praise Him with the lute [flute]": Meaning the tongue, which is a flute for the Lord.
  • "Praise Him with the harp": Which is the mouth when the Holy Spirit moves it like a string.
  • "Praise Him with the timbrel [tambourine]": This points to the Church meditating on the Resurrection from the dead through striking the skin of the dead drum.
  • "Praise Him with stringed instruments and flutes [organs]": The organ is the body, and its nerves are the strings played upon.
  • "Praise Him with loud cymbals": This points to the lips of the mouth when the sounds of melodies come forth from them.
  • "Let everything that has breath praise the Lord": Here he calls all humanity to praise, because our Lord cares for every creature that breathes.

And in the Apostolic Constitutions (Canon 78) it says:

"As long as the Holy Mysteries are on the Altar before they are lifted up, the reading before Him shall not cease; rather, they shall chant the word of God or say the Psalm... Praise does not cease until it is finished."

Washing the Vessels (Al-Awani)

After Communion (Al-Tanawul) is finished, the Father Priest begins washing the vessels. He makes absolutely sure that there are no particles (Gawahir) on the Paten (Siniya) or inside the Chalice (Kass). The Serving Deacon at the Altar (Al-Mazbah) also confirms that there are no particles in the Paten.

Abouna begins to wash the Chalice from the inside, along with the Spoon (Masteer / Mali'qah), and drinks the water. Then he washes the Chalice from the inside and outside, pours the water into the Paten, and drinks it. If he used his finger to commune a small infant, he washes his fingers in the Chalice and drinks the water [6].

Then Abouna washes the Spoon with water, gives it to the Partner Priest, and touches his eyes with it—this custom has existed in the Coptic Church for over 10 centuries. Then he washes the edges of the Dome (Qubba / Star) and drinks it as well. Then he puts water in the Paten for the deacons who served in the Sanctuary (Al-Haykal) to drink from it.

The vessels are left for the deacons to dry them. They gather the Veils (Lafa'if) and place them neatly on a clean Covering (Sitr). The deacon ties the covering with five knots.

So the number of knots is five. Why? Because when Abouna sets the Altar before the Liturgy begins, he makes three signs (Rushumat) and unties the three knots, then makes two signs and unties the two knots [7].

Post-Communion Prayers & Dismissal

The Prayer of Thanksgiving after Communion: Abouna begins the Prayer of Thanksgiving with the Paten (Siniya) placed on his head. This means that all the prayers of the Liturgy surpass all human thought [8].

"Our mouth is filled with joy, and our tongue with gladness, for our partaking of Your immortal mysteries, O Lord. For that which eye has not seen, nor ear heard, nor has entered into the heart of man, the things which You, O God, have prepared for those who love Your Holy Name..."

The Prayer of Submission to the Father (Salat Al-Khudou' lil-Aab): Abouna places the Paten on the throne (Kursi) of the Chalice, raises his hands, spreads them, and says:

"Your servants, O Lord, who serve You and seek Your Holy Name and submit to You. Dwell within them, rejoice among them, help them in every good work... through Christ Jesus our Lord."

At this point, we and all Your people cry out, saying: "Have mercy on us, O God our Savior." [9]

Dismissing the Angel of the Sacrifice (Malaak Al-Zabiha): The deacon pours water into the priest's hand. Abouna stands before the Altar and dismisses the Angel of the Sacrifice by sprinkling a little water on the Altar, and then throwing the rest upward while saying:

"O angel of this sacrifice, flying up to the highest with this praise, remember us before the Lord, that He may forgive us our sins." [10]

Everyone in the church responds, saying: "Glory to God in the highest, and on earth peace, goodwill toward men."

The Dismissal (Al-Tasreeh): After dismissing the Angel of the Sacrifice, Abouna begins giving the Dismissal. He places his wet hand on the beards of the father priests present, as a sign of respect for their consecration [11]. As for the rest of the people, he gives them the Dismissal by sprinkling water on them in an orderly manner. This is a sign that the people have been filled with the Holy Spirit as a result of Communion.

When Abouna finishes sprinkling the water, he returns towards the Sanctuary (Al-Haykal) and says: "Make us worthy to say thankfully: Our Father who art in heaven..." At its end, he gives the Dismissal to the people, saying:

"The love of God the Father the Pantocrator, the grace of the Only-Begotten Son, our Lord, God, and Savior Jesus Christ, and the communion, gift, and fellowship of the Holy Spirit be with you all. Go in peace; the peace of the Lord be with you." [12]

The Psalm of Joy (Mazmour Al-Farah): You will notice that Abouna enters the Sanctuary, clapping his hands and kissing the corners of the Altar, while reciting Psalm 47 [13]: "Oh, clap your hands, all you peoples! Shout to God with the voice of triumph!"

After he circles the Altar and kisses its four corners, Abouna closes the curtain of the Sanctuary, saying: "O Lord, cast Your veil over us, and make the door of Your Church and congregation open before our faces..."

The Bread of Blessing (Luqmat Al-Baraka / Eulogia): He goes out to distribute the bread of blessing, sometimes called the Eulogia (Al-Oulogiya), which is a Greek word meaning "Blessing." This is distributed from the remaining Oblation (Qurban) [14]. After finishing the distribution of the bread, the priest takes off his service vestments.

With this, I have finished with you the detailed explanation of the Divine Liturgy (Al-Quddas Al-Ilahi). I thank God that He helped me to simplify all the meanings, words, and movements. Amen.

Footnotes

[1] The Flaming Sword: Referencing Genesis 3:24. The deacon holding the candle and cross represents the Cherub whose flaming sword barred humanity from the Tree of Life, but now steps aside. ↩
[2] Carrying the Paten to the People: Showing the Paten twice represents the resurrected Christ appearing to His disciples and then ascending into Heaven. ↩
[3] Confession before Communion: The moment of receiving the Eucharist is not the time for the Sacrament of Confession. Confession should be done beforehand. ↩
[4] Saint John Cassian: Encourages frequent communion as spiritual medicine rather than a reward for the perfect. ↩
[5] Clement of Alexandria: Taught that the true "instruments" of worship in the New Covenant are our own physical bodies praising God. ↩
[6] Washing the Vessels: Ensures that no trace of the consecrated Body and Blood is left behind. ↩
[7] The Five Knots: Represent the five wounds of Christ. Untying them at the beginning of the Liturgy prepares for the sacrifice. ↩
[8] The Paten on the Head: A gesture of submission, acknowledging the mystery is higher than human logic. ↩
[9] A Plea for Intercession: A direct plea for divine help, highlighting the Church's communal dependence on God's mercy. ↩
[10] The Angel of the Sacrifice: formally dismissing the angel assigned to attend every Divine Liturgy. ↩
[11] The Priest's Beard: A symbol of consecration drawing from the Old Testament priesthood and early desert fathers. ↩
[12] The Apostolic Blessing: Drawn from 2 Corinthians 13:14, sending the believers out with the peace of the Holy Trinity. ↩
[13] Psalm Numbering: Psalm 46 (Coptic) / Psalm 47 (NKJV). Clapping is a sign of joy. ↩
[14] Reverence for the Eulogia: Though not the Body of Christ, this bread is treated with respect as it was present in the Sanctuary. ↩
