1 - Raising of Incense for Matins & Vespers

The prayers of the Raising of Incense (Tasbeha) for Vespers (Ashia) and Matins (Baker) are among the prayers that the Church pays close attention to. Of course, the Divine Liturgy (Al-Quddas Al-Ilahi) is the most wonderful journey the Church takes us on to Heaven. In the Divine Liturgy, we partake in glory and joy, and it is supposed to inform, enlighten, and make every person know about the life of Heaven. This is why in the prayer of the Third Hour, in the last section, we say:

"When we stand in Your holy sanctuary, we are considered as standing in Heaven."

And as we praise, we always remember Heaven. The Raising of Incense for Vespers and Matins is a prelude to the prayers of the Divine Liturgy. We notice that all prayers, whether in Vespers, Matins, or the Liturgy, are chanted melodically, even a simple phrase like: "Peace be with all of you."[1]

The prayers of Vespers and Matins are the preparation for receiving the King of Kings and Lord of Lords in the Divine Liturgy, when the bread is transformed into His pure Body and the wine into His precious Blood. The Divine Liturgy is a miniature picture of the life of Heaven, because in Heaven there is perpetual, unceasing praise. Our faith always holds that Heaven is constant praise to God. Our Priest (Abouna)[2] says in the Liturgy: "You have given those on earth the praise of the Seraphim."

We also notice that the Sanctuary (Al-Haykal)[3] is at a higher level than the rest of the church because it represents the Holy of Holies[4]. And the Choir of Deacons (Khóros Al-Shamāmesa)[5] is higher than the nave of the church. This is because the Church wants to say that the Clergy (Al-Ikleerus)[6] are on a higher level spiritually and must be at a high spiritual standard. This doesn't mean they are better than other people; no, they are servants of God first, and secondly, servants to you. It is also because the clergy appear before God in the prayers of the Church. Take note that the Raising of Incense for Vespers and Matins is performed outside the Sanctuary. Why? Because the Altar of Incense (Madhbah Al-Bukhur)[7] was outside the Holy of Holies in the Tabernacle. That's why we notice Abouna praying and standing outside the Sanctuary, whether it's Vespers or Matins. And Abouna prays with his head uncovered before the Lord, as a sign that the priest, when praying before God, is exposed before Him. Certainly, each one of us has mistakes and weaknesses, but at this moment, he is exposed: "Before You, O Lord, and before You, nothing is hidden from You." Also, in the Book of Revelation, it speaks of the twenty-four elders: "...casting their crowns before the throne."[8] This is a form of reverence and solemnity. Therefore, at this moment, the person, whether the priest or the deacon, stands with reverence and solemnity because they are standing before the Holy of Holies, before the Divine Altar.

The Prayer of Matins (Baker) or Vespers (Ashia) Begins With the Opening of the Curtain (Al-Sitr)

Abouna holds the Cross in his hand and opens the curtain from left to right. Why? Because through the Cross, the door of Heaven was opened to us once again, and through the Cross, reconciliation occurred between the earthly and the heavenly. As Abouna opens the curtain, he says: "Have mercy upon us, O God the Father the Pantocrator (Dabit Al-Kol)[9], O Holy Trinity have mercy on us, O Lord, God of Hosts, be with us, for we have no other helper in our hardships and tribulations but You." Furthermore, when Abouna opens the curtain from left to right, this signifies that God restored us from the "left" state to the "right" state[10]. Every movement has a meaning derived from the Church Fathers, the received teaching (Al-Tasleem), and Tradition (Al-Taqleed). After that, Abouna offers a prostration (Metania) before the Sanctuary and before the congregation. Then, he begins the Prayer of Thanksgiving (Salat Al-Shukr), which is the introduction to all Church prayers. Abouna begins by addressing the deacon, saying: "Pray." (Eshlil)[12]. The deacon then conveys this: "Stand up for prayer." (Epi prosevki stathite). At the end of the Prayer of Thanksgiving, Abouna adds the appropriate phrase for the occasion, such as for the Lakkan[11].

Why the Right Side of the Altar?

Abouna stands to the right of the Altar (Al-Mazbah), representing power and how Zacharias the Priest was standing at the right side of the Altar of Incense when the angel appeared. Abouna places five hands[13] of incense. The First Hand: "In the name of the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit, One God, Amen." The Second Sign (Rashm)[14]: "Blessed is God the Father the Pantocrator, Amen." The Third Sign: "Blessed is His Only-Begotten Son..." The Fourth Sign: "Blessed is the Holy Spirit..." The Fifth Hand: "Glory and honor..." Every time Abouna makes these signs, the deacon must say "Amen." Then, Abouna prays inaudibly (Sirran)[15] during Vespers: "O Christ our God, the Great, the Awesome, the True, the Only-Begotten Son, Word of God the Father. A poured-out ointment is Your Holy Name, and in every place incense is offered to Your Holy Name and a pure offering."[16] The deacon responds inaudibly: "Pray for our sacrifice and for those who offered it." In Matins, Abouna prays: "O God, who accepted unto Him the offerings of Abel the Just, the sacrifice of Noah and Abraham, and the incense of Aaron and Zacharias."

The Three Short Litanies (Al-Awashi Al-Sighar)

He prays the Three Short Litanies[17] while circling the Altar. 1. The Litany of Peace: "Remember, O Lord, the peace of Your One, Only, Holy, Catholic[18], and Apostolic Church." He moves to the South (Qibli)[19] side. 2. The Litany of the Fathers: Facing West, he says: "Remember, O Lord, our Patriarch... Pope Anba Tawadros II, and... Metropolitan Anba Serapion."[20] 3. The Litany of the Assemblies: Facing East, he says: "Remember, O Lord, our assemblies; bless them." He incenses in the four directions and prays: "But let Your people be in blessing, thousands of thousands and ten thousands[21] of ten thousands doing Your will." Reach the West of the Altar, face towards the East, he says: "Through the grace, compassion, and love of mankind of Your Only-Begotten Son..."

The Incense Cross (Saleeb Al-Bukhur)

Abouna performs the Incense Cross three times. He incenses North (Bahari)[22] for the Virgin Mary, West for the Apostles and Saints, and South for John the Baptist. In Vespers, he prays the Litany of the Departed. This is prayed regardless of the tune (Joyful or Annual)[23]. In Matins, he prays for the Sick and Travelers. After this, he greets the Gospel at the Lectern (Manjalia)[24] and gives a hand of incense to his brother priest. Then comes the Procession among the people, circling counter-clockwise[25]. Everyone offers their prayers, gathered in the Mystery of Return (Sirr Al-Raj'aa)[26]. He prays the Reverent Verses (Al-Arba' Al-Khushu'iyyat)[27] acknowledging Jesus as the One Hypostasis (Oqnum)[28].

The Conclusion of the Prayers

The deacons begin "We Exalt You," followed by the Creed. Abouna signs the people and begins the Supplication (Efnoti Nai Nan)[29]. Finally, he begins the Litany of the Gospel, carrying the Gospel (Bishara)[30] and reciting the prayer of Simeon the Elder[31]: "Lord, now You are letting Your servant depart in peace, According to Your word; For my eyes have seen Your salvation Which You have prepared before the face of all peoples, A light to bring revelation to the Gentiles, And the glory of Your people Israel." After that, Abouna incenses the Gospel three times, the priests kiss the Gospel, the Gospel is read, and the service concludes with the Absolution and Dismissal.
