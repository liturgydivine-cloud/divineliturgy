The Prayers of the Rite

The Divine Liturgy (Al-Quddas) is a very important thing in our lives because the Liturgy is the meeting with God and the union with Him in Communion (Al-Tanawul). Furthermore, the Liturgy is an invitation from God to attend the Heavenly Wedding, the Marriage Supper of the Lamb [1].

That is why I will speak with you about the explanation of the Rite (Taqs) of the Liturgy, so that we may understand what is happening before us, and also so that we may pray with the spirit, pray with the body, and with the mind. We truly want to leave the Liturgy filled with the Spirit of God, the grace of God, and our union with God, because He said:

"He who eats My flesh and drinks My blood abides in Me, and I in him." (John 6:56)

The Mystery of the Eucharist (Sirr Al-Eucharistia) [2] or the Liturgy means the mystery of Christ’s permanent presence in the Church, and consequently the presence of the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit, because They are One. In other words, the Mystery of the Eucharist is the secret of our arrival to God.

Aside from that, the Divine Liturgy is an exit from this world and an ascension to Heaven. The Holy Altar (Al-Mazbah) is the axis of this elevation and lifting up above worldly matters. It is the secret of the survival of Eastern Christianity until today, despite the challenges and persecutions against the Church throughout the ages. Because the Church is the ship of salvation for the peoples from the flood of this world. The Church of Alexandria specifically has a history that clarifies the extent of the persecution it faced throughout the ages, and the secret to its survival is the Divine Liturgy and the Daily Praise (Tasbeha); they are the secret of the Church's preservation until now, and it will remain by the power of its Lord and the indwelling of God amidst His people in every Liturgy.

We must always be careful in our speech when I say, "We are coming to the Liturgy." I come to pray the Liturgy, not to attend the Liturgy. There is a big difference [3].

Because when you pray, you are coming to appear before God, to pray, to be in the presence of God, and to live the story of salvation and redemption. Then, in the end, you receive the Body and Blood of Christ worthily. But to say you "attend" the Liturgy is wrong. Anyone can "attend" the Liturgy on social media—is that called presence? I apologize for the expression, but the Pews (Banshat) and the Chandeliers (Najaf) in the church are "attending" the Liturgy. But you, as a Christian person, are coming to pray the Liturgy and live moments of eternity.

The most beautiful and creative thing in the Liturgy, which is Communion (Al-Tanawul), is the popular presence of men, women, boys, girls, young men, young women, children, and elders, all gathering around Christ the Savior, the Beloved, the Owner of the House and the Lord of the House, who invites us all to His food and drink, which is Communion—the Bread of Life coming down from Heaven. Is there anything greater or more beautiful than this Mystery which surpasses the understanding and thinking of the limited human mind?

Why We Must Understand the Prayers of the Rite

When we understand the prayers of the Rite (Taqs) of the Liturgy, we know why our Priest (Abouna) says:

"O Great and Eternal God,"

and why we say:

"Through the intercessions of the Theotokos." [4]

We understand why we say "Lord have mercy" (Kyrie Eleison) so many times in the Liturgy, and what is happening in the Liturgy and its Rite. Why does Abouna sometimes hold the Censer (Shorya), sometimes cover his hands with the Veil (Lafa'if), sometimes leave them uncovered, sometimes look at the people and give them a blessing, and sometimes prostrate (Yasjud)? And why do we also prostrate during the Liturgy at specifically those times? For all these reasons, we must understand the prayers of the Liturgy.

Of course, when we understand the Liturgy and know what is in the prayers and what is happening in front of us, we become more engaged and live the prayers of the Liturgy deeply. Because if I am just standing there listening to Abouna's prayers, or the congregation's or deacons' prayers, and repeating them without understanding, there will inevitably come a time when I want the time to pass quickly. I might talk to the person next to me, check my mobile phone, or sometimes the worshiper might even leave the church.

This also causes many people to come late, saying, "The Liturgy is all the same anyway." No, of course, the Liturgy is not all the same. The sanctification and the Liturgy are not uniform throughout. The beginning is different from the end; the first half-hour of the Liturgy is different from the last half-hour, and different from the end of the Liturgy.

There are many things that, once we know them, our only concern will be to attend the Liturgy from the very beginning—from the moment Abouna opens the Sanctuary Curtain (Sitr Al-Haykal). That is why we must understand the Rite of the Liturgy and its prayers. Opening the curtain means Heaven is open before us, and the Throne of God is before our eyes, which is the Sanctuary and the Holy Table (the Altar / Al-Mazbah) [5].

Furthermore, when we learn, we teach our grandchildren what the Church is and what the Liturgy is. Those present with us who have children should teach them what the Church is and what the Liturgy is. We bring our children to church so they may pray, learn how to pray, and be attached to the Church from a young age. Because the Church is the place that teaches prayer, just like school teaches them reading and writing. Every time they come to church, they learn something new.

Of course, the person who truly lives the Liturgy and its prayers will receive many things. It is enough that after Communion (Al-Tanawul), they should leave the church joyful, filled with peace, love, and calmness of soul. Truly, if you find someone with these characteristics, you can say, "This person truly lived the Liturgy and prayed the Liturgy with all their heart." The opposite is true: if someone leaves frowning, not wanting to talk to anyone, just wanting to leave—what a loss! They did not receive anything.

I will speak to you about the explanation of the Liturgy in a very simple way. I will not go into precise details unless necessary, because precise details concern the Fathers the Priests and the Deacons, as they are the servants of this Mystery.

The Structure of the Liturgy on Our Ten Fingers

Let us begin with the Liturgy and memorize its structure on our ten fingers so that it becomes easy.

First, we divide the Liturgy into two main sections:

  1. The First Section: The Liturgy of the Catechumens (Quddas Al-Maw'uzin) [6] or the Liturgy of the Word (Al-Quddas Al-Ta'limi).
  2. The Second Section: The Liturgy of the Faithful (Quddas Al-Mu'minin).

(Note: This distinction existed in the early Church. But now, the Liturgy of the Word and the Liturgy of the Faithful are considered one Liturgy because everyone attends both; there are no longer catechumens who are dismissed early).

  • The Liturgy of the Word: We divide it into 3 parts.
  • The Liturgy of the Faithful: We divide it into 6 parts.

So where is the tenth part? This is independent; it is the part of Communion (Al-Tanawul). So, the tenth finger belongs to Communion.

Before I speak about each point, we must know that the Liturgy and Communion are very important in our lives. The person who partakes of Communion must be baptized and anointed with the Holy Myron (Al-Mayrun) to be able to partake of these Holy Things (the Body and Blood of the Lord Jesus Christ).

What does the word "Liturgy" (Quddas) mean and where does it come from? Very simply, it means that the Holy God descends upon the Oblations (Al-Qarabeen) placed on the Holy Altar, and they transform into Holies (Qudsat—the Holy Body and Precious Blood). And when we partake of them and receive them, we become Saints (Qiddisin).

How does this happen? We will know when we explain the nine steps in which the bread and wine are transformed into Holies. And we, sinful humans, are transformed into saints because the Holy God is present with us. We unite with Him because we have been sanctified by the prayers of the Liturgy. That is why Abouna comes at the end of the Liturgy and says: "The Holies for the Holy" (Ta agia tis agiois), because we have truly been sanctified by our prayers in the Liturgy, and we are also sanctified when we partake of the Body and Blood of the Lord. "He who eats My flesh and drinks My blood abides in Me, and I in him."

The beautiful thing in the Church is that the first thing you find during the distribution of the Mysteries is the deacons chanting Psalm 150. While the NKJV Bible translates it as: "Praise God in His sanctuary," in the Coptic Church, based on the ancient translation, we chant: "Praise God in all His saints. Alleluia." Who are the saints? You. Those who have been sanctified by Communion.

The Liturgy of the Word (or Catechumens): We divide it into 3 points:

  1. The Prayer of the Psalms (Agpeya): On Sundays, we pray the Third and Sixth Hours. On weekdays, we pray from the Third to the Ninth Hour. In Great Lent, we pray from the Third Hour to the Compline (Al-Nom).
  2. The Choosing of the Lamb (Ikhtiyar Al-Hamal) and the Signs of the Lamb.
  3. The Readings in the Liturgy:
    • a) The Pauline Epistle (Al-Bolis)
    • b) The Catholic Epistle (Al-Catholicon)
    • c) The Acts of the Apostles (Al-Praxis)
    • d) The Synaxarium (Al-Synaxar) [7]
    • e) The Gospel. After the Gospel comes the Sermon (Al-Wa'za).

After that, the Creed (Qanun Al-Iman) is recited. This must be said audibly, and the whole church says it. After the Creed, the Liturgy of the Faithful begins.

We divide the Liturgy of the Faithful into six points:

To make them easy, we divide these six points into two groups of three:

The First Three Parts are:

  1. The Prayer of Reconciliation (Salat Al-Solh): Abouna begins it by saying, "O Great and Eternal God."
  2. "Meet and Right" (Mostahaq wa Adel): This begins with "It is meet and right", where he speaks about the angels.
  3. "Agios": This begins with "Agios" or "Holy, Holy, Holy." In this part, he speaks to God, and it ends with the Sanctification (Al-Taqdis), where the bread is transformed into the Body of the Lord Jesus Christ and the wine into His Blood.

The Last Three Points (The Church in Christ):

  1. The Litanies (Al-Awashi): The Seven Short Litanies.
  2. The Commemoration of the Saints (Al-Magma'): The Victorious Church.
  3. The Fraction (Al-Qisma).

And Point Number 10:

  • Communion (Al-Tanawul).

So, we have finished the ten points; we know them now. I will speak about each point in detail. Of course, every part of the Liturgy and every step differs from the other until we reach the meeting with the Bridegroom and partake of His Body and Blood worthily. May God give us grace so that every time we attend the Liturgy, we take something new, a new grace, a new power, and a new spiritual life, and march in the procession of victory and triumph with our Good Savior. Amen.

Footnotes

[1] Marriage Supper of the Lamb: This refers to Revelation 19:9. The Liturgy is viewed as a foretaste of this eternal heavenly banquet. ↩
[2] Eucharist: From the Greek word Eucharistia, meaning "Thanksgiving." It is the central sacrament of the Church. ↩
[3] Pray vs. Attend: A crucial distinction. "Praying" implies active participation—joining in the hymns and responses. ↩
[4] Theotokos: A Greek title for the Virgin Mary, meaning "God-bearer" or "Mother of God." ↩
[5] Opening the Curtain: Symbolizes the opening of Heaven to humanity through Christ. The altar represents God's throne. ↩
[6] Catechumens (Maw'uzin): People learning the faith but not yet baptized, who heard the readings but left before the Eucharist. ↩
[7] Synaxarium: The book containing the lives of the saints and martyrs read daily to inspire the faithful. ↩
