The Holy Gospel

Today, by the grace of God, I will speak with you about the final reading in the Liturgy of the Word (Al-Quddas Al-Ta'limi), which is the Holy Gospel (Al-Ingil Al-Muqaddas).

This reading is considered the crown of all the previous readings [1]. It is the words that illuminate for us and guide us to the path of the Kingdom, and the light of the days of our pilgrimage on earth. Because they are the words of the Lord of Glory, Jesus Christ, on the lips of the one who reads the Gospel, which fill our hearts with comfort and joy in times of sorrow, and are a balm for all our sicknesses.

Why do we read the Gospel at the end of the readings and before the sermon? And what is the importance of the Gospel in my life?

  1. It is food for the spirit:
    "Your words were found, and I ate them." (Jeremiah 15:16). "How sweet are Your words to my taste, sweeter than honey to my mouth! ... I rejoice at Your word as one who finds great treasure." (Psalm 119:103, 162). "Today, if you will hear His voice, do not harden your hearts." (Hebrews 3:15). [2]
  2. It gives purity to the soul, body, and spirit: As the Lord of Glory said:
    "You are already clean because of the word which I have spoken to you." (John 15:3).
  3. It illuminates the path before us:
    "The entrance of Your words gives light; it gives understanding to the simple." (Psalm 119:130). "Your word is a lamp to my feet and a light to my path." (Psalm 119:105).
  4. The Gospel is medicine for all diseases of the spirit: It convicts us of sin, reminds us of God's promises, and teaches us how to deal with God. The Gospel is profitable for teaching because it contains the sayings of God and the teachings of the Lord of Glory.

Of course, the Gospel is our best and holiest teacher in our spiritual life. It is the constitution of our lives, keeping us away from anger, hatred, revenge, swearing, idle talk, and every sin that separates me from God.

How to Read the Gospel & Prepare Myself

I will pause from explaining the Liturgy to speak with you about how to read the Gospel and prepare myself for it, because you will be in the presence of God.

God wants from us the tithe (Ushur) of the day. This is His right over us, to divide this time between reading the Gospel and prayer.

The best time to read the Gospel is in the morning, in the early hours of the day, because your mind is clear, your body is energetic, and it is before the preoccupations of the world and the day. The best time to start with God is the first hours of the day: you read the Gospel and then pray, so you start your day with God, and He will bless the entire day. Because God said to Moses:

"So be ready in the morning, and come up in the morning to Mount Sinai, and present yourself to Me there on the top of the mountain." (Exodus 34:2).

Of course, everyone knows the suitable time for their circumstances to read the Gospel. Just as we have the desire to eat, drink, rest, and sleep, the same applies to the Gospel. You must have the desire to open and read the Gospel whenever you have free time. Keep this desire to open the Holy Bible and read it. You will find new truths every time you open the Gospel. Just like treasures that require effort to discover, the same applies to the Holy Bible; it requires depth and effort to read deeply, not superficially. For example, memorizing and studying one verse is more beneficial and better than reading a whole chapter superficially without understanding.

Studying the Holy Bible with a spirit of simplicity and humility: The more you humble yourself before God while reading the Gospel—not claiming wisdom and understanding, but at the same time asking God to teach you and declaring your ignorance before Him—grace begins to work, and the Word of God begins to work in your heart. Because God revealed His mysteries to babes when He said:

"I thank You, Father, Lord of heaven and earth, that You have hidden these things from the wise and prudent and have revealed them to babes." (Matthew 11:25).

Pray before reading the Gospel: Because you need God, the Author of the Gospel, and His guidance. Pray the Psalm of David and say:

"Open my eyes, that I may see wondrous things from Your law." (Psalm 119:18).

And ask God for the guidance of His Holy Spirit to understand His word. When reading the Gospel, consider that the words you are reading are directed to you personally. Have this feeling that you are in the presence of God. Study the Word of God with reverence and trust in everything you read, and in God's promises, no matter how great they are. Accept the teaching of the Bible without debate, even if it seems impossible or unreasonable, because with God, all things are possible.

Memorize the greatest possible number of verses to help you against the thoughts and tricks of the enemy of good. For example:

  • Against lust: "Your word I have hidden in my heart, that I might not sin against You" (Psalm 119:11), and "How then can I do this great wickedness...?" (Genesis 39:9).
  • The power of the Gospel is effective against the enemy of good: As our teacher John said, "I have written to you, young men, because you are strong, and the word of God abides in you, and you have overcome the wicked one." (1 John 2:14). Because we are truly strong by the Word of God when it abides in us.
  • In asking for relief and help: Say with Isaiah the Prophet: "That I should know how to speak a word in season to him who is weary..." (Isaiah 50:4). [3]

Everything you read in the Gospel, apply it to yourself. Make the Holy Bible the mirror that reveals your soul before it and corrects it. Also, memorizing verses helps with prayer, gives you strength and zeal, guides you to God's promises and ways, and fills your heart with comfort and joy in times of sorrow.

The Hymn of the Angels: The Trisagion (Al-Thalath Taqdisat)

Let's return to the explanation of the Liturgy: After reading the Synaxarium, the deacons and the people begin to chant the Hymn of the Angels, which is the Trisagion (Al-Thalath Taqdisat / Holy, Holy, Holy). Of course, to explain where the Church got the subject of the Trisagion is an independent topic in itself, but I will say a few simple things.

  • The first one to speak about it was Isaiah the Prophet when he saw God sitting on a high throne, and the train of His robe filled the temple, and before Him the angels were saying: "Holy, holy, holy is the Lord of hosts; the whole earth is full of His glory!" (Isaiah 6:3).
  • Also, in the Book of Revelation, when John saw before the throne a sea of glass, like crystal, and the four incorporeal living creatures who do not rest day or night, saying: "Holy, holy, holy, Lord God Almighty, who was and is and is to come!" (Revelation 4:8).
  • Then there is the story of Nicodemus during the burial of the Lord Jesus Christ. He marvelled at His death and was astounded: How could the Prince of Life die? He who raised Lazarus? He whom the Father bore witness to from Heaven? Nicodemus said, "Where is Your might, O Lord?" The Lord Jesus Christ turned to him, and immediately he heard the angels of Heaven saying: "Holy God, Holy Mighty, Holy Immortal." And Nicodemus cried out and said: "O You who were crucified for us, have mercy on us!" [4]

The Trisagion entered the Liturgy at the end of the third century, after the Council of Nicaea. And of course, you know the Trisagion:

"...O You who were born of the Virgin, have mercy on us."
"...O You who were crucified for us, have mercy on us."
"...O You who rose from the dead, have mercy on us."

The Litany of the Gospel (Oushiat Al-Ingil)

At the end of the Trisagion, Abouna stands before the Altar. The Serving Deacon [5] presents the Censer (Shorya) to him, and he places one hand of incense in it. He stands before the Sanctuary and begins the prayer of the Litany of the Gospel (Oushiat Al-Ingil). The deacon stands behind him carrying the Gospel (Bishara) and the Cross.

Abouna begins the prayer by saying:

"O Master, Lord Jesus Christ our God, who said to His saintly, honored disciples and pure apostles, 'Many prophets and righteous men have desired to see what you see, and have not seen it, and to hear what you hear, and have not heard it. But blessed are your eyes for they see, and your ears for they hear.' May we be worthy to hear and to act according to Your Holy Gospels through the prayers of Your saints."
  • The deacon carrying the Cross and the Gospel (Bishara) responds and says: "Pray for the Holy Gospel."
  • The people and the deacons say: "Lord have mercy."

After that, Abouna completes the prayer, asking God to remember those who have bidden us to remember them in our supplications. He remembers those who have fallen asleep and asks for their repose, and he remembers the sick and asks for their healing. He completes the prayer, saying:

"For You are the life of us all, the salvation of us all, the hope of us all, the healing of us all, and the resurrection of us all."

Then he incenses towards the Gospel, and the deacon enters along with Abouna, carrying the Gospel (Bishara) and the Cross. Abouna places a hand of incense in the censer. He circles around the Altar while praying the Prayer of Simeon the Elder:

"Lord, now You are letting Your servant depart in peace, according to Your word; for my eyes have seen Your salvation which You have prepared before the face of all peoples, a light to bring revelation to the Gentiles, and the glory of Your people Israel."

If there are other priests, he gives them the Gospel (Bishara) to kiss it, saying: "Blessed are your eyes for they see." The priests kiss the Gospel (Bishara) while saying: "Worship the Gospel of Jesus Christ."

The Reading of the Gospel

The deacon stands at the door of the Sanctuary (Al-Haykal), raising the Cross, and says: "Stand in the fear of God and listen to the Holy Gospel."

Abouna places the Gospel (Bishara) on his head and says: "Blessed is He who comes in the name of the Lord of Hosts. O Lord, bless the portion of the Holy Gospel," and he mentions the name of the Gospel writer (Matthew, Mark, Luke, or John). The people and the deacons respond: "Glory to You, O Lord."

Candles are lit on the right and left of the Lectern (Manjalia), signifying that the Word of God is the Light of the world, and He came as the Light of the world ("Your word is a lamp to my feet and a light to my path." - Psalm 119:105).

The reader of the Gospel steps forward and says: "Once again, stand in the fear of God and listen to the Holy Gospel, a reading from the Gospel according to our teacher [Mentions the Gospel writer]."

I want you to notice how many times we are alerted to stand and listen to the Gospel with fear and trembling, whether by the deacon or by Abouna. Because the Speaker is truly the Lord of Glory, not the Gospel reader.

The Secret Prayers During the Gospel

During this time, you will find Abouna has entered the Sanctuary (Al-Haykal) and begun to pray inaudibly (Sirran) while holding the censer. He says this deep prayer [6]:

"O longsuffering, abundant in mercy, and true: receive our supplications and our requests from us. Accept our supplications, our repentance, and our confession on Your holy, undefiled, heavenly altar, that we may be made worthy to hear Your Holy Gospels, and keep Your commandments and Your commands, and bring forth fruit therein, a hundredfold, sixtyfold, and thirtyfold, in Christ Jesus our Lord.

Remember, O Lord, the sick of Your people; visit them with mercies and compassions, heal them. Remember, O Lord, our fathers and brethren who are traveling; bring them back to their own countries in peace and safety. Remember, O Lord, the seeds, the herbs, the plants of the field, and the waters of the rivers; bless them. Remember, O Lord, the salvation of men and beasts. Remember, O Lord, the salvation of this Your holy place and all places, and all the monasteries of our Orthodox fathers. Remember, O Lord, Your servant the ruler of our land; keep him in peace, justice, and might. Remember, O Lord, the captives; deliver them all. Remember, O Lord, our fathers and brethren who have fallen asleep and reposed in the Orthodox faith; repose all their souls. Remember, O Lord, those who have brought unto You these gifts, those on whose behalf they have been brought, and those by whom they have been brought; give them all the heavenly reward. Remember, O Lord, those who are distressed in hardships and tribulations; save them from all their hardships.

Remember, O Lord, the catechumens of Your people; have mercy upon them and confirm them in faith in You. All remaining idol worship, remove from their hearts; Your law, Your fear, Your commandments, Your statutes, Your holy commands, confirm in their hearts. Grant them to know the certainty of the words in which they were instructed, that in the appointed time they may be worthy of the washing of the new birth for the remission of their sins, making them a temple for Your Holy Spirit; through the grace, compassions, and love of mankind of Your Only-Begotten Son, our Lord, God, and Savior, Jesus Christ, to Whom is due glory and honor forever. Amen."

The Prayer of the Veil (Salat Al-Higab)

After the reading of the Gospel, and after Abouna finishes this prayer, the Sermon begins. (After the Sermon in the early Church, the catechumens would leave, the doors of the church would be closed, and only the faithful would remain to complete the Liturgy / the Anaphora). After the Sermon, the Gospel Response is chanted.

During this time, Abouna prays the Prayer of the Veil (Salat Al-Higab), saying:

"O God, who, for Your unspeakable love to mankind, sent Your Only-Begotten Son into the world to bring back unto You the lost sheep, we entreat You, O our Master, not to cast us behind You as we place our hands upon this awesome and unbloody sacrifice. For we do not trust in our own righteousness, but in Your mercy by which You have given life to our race.

We ask and entreat Your goodness, O Lover of Mankind, that this mystery which You have ordained for our salvation may not be unto condemnation for us or for any of Your people, but for the blotting out of our sins, the forgiveness of our negligence, and for glory and honor to Your Holy Name, O Father, Son, and Holy Spirit, now and at all times and unto the ages of all ages. Amen."

Notice that Reverend Abouna, from the moment he enters the door of the church until the end of the Liturgy, is in a state of continuous prayer. May God accept their holy prayers on our behalf. Amen. To our God be all glory and honor forever. Amen.
