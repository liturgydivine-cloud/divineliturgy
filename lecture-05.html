The Catholic Epistle

Review

Last time, we learned where the Church took the idea of the Liturgy of the Word (Al-Quddas Al-Ta'limi)—from the readings of the Pauline Epistle to the Psalm and the Gospel. Of course, we learned that it was taken from the story of the two disciples of Emmaus.

Of course, now there is no such thing as the "Liturgy of the Catechumens" [in the sense of dismissing them], but there is the Liturgy of the Word, which concerns every one of us, and I explained this last time.

So, what is the benefit of the readings we hear, or the Liturgy of the Word?

This prepares everyone in the church—every person present in the church, from the Father Priest to the deacons and all the people. When we hear the words of our teacher Paul the Apostle in his epistles that are read to us, or from the Catholic Epistle (Al-Catholicon), which are the General Epistles (as we will learn today), and then the Acts of the Apostles (Al-Praxis)—we learn how the pure Apostles in the early Church, by the power and grace of the Holy Spirit, spread the faith in the world.

After that, the Synaxarium (Al-Synaxar) is read. It is a Greek word meaning "News" or "Compilations" [1], meaning the history of the fathers, prophets, patriarchs, bishops, priests, deacons, martyrs, saints, and confessors who preserved the faith.

Why is the Synaxarium read? To show us how much our holy fathers preserved the faith and handed it down to us pure, and so that we may follow their footsteps, walk in the same path, complete the journey, and preserve the faith they handed down to us pure and sound. Meaning, they are an example for us. It is also a continuation of the Book of Acts.

(Take note: The Book of Acts has not ended yet, and it will not end with the word "Amen." Meaning, the book is still open because the Church is still carrying out the work of preaching until now, and is still offering martyrs and saints. It is still standing by the power of the Holy Spirit and the Chief Cornerstone, the Lord Jesus Christ).

As we will learn from Dr. Medhat, the Book of Acts ended with two extremely wondrous verses, saying:

"Then Paul dwelt two whole years in his own rented house, and received all who came to him, preaching the kingdom of God and teaching the things which concern the Lord Jesus Christ with all confidence, no one forbidding him." (Acts 28:30-31).

The book did not end with the word "Amen," and it remains open until the Second Coming.

This is God's word to us and to everyone present in the church. We must listen to it with attentive ears, understanding, and an open heart and mind. Why? Because these words purify us, for they are the words of the Lord Jesus Christ to us. He confirmed this when He said:

"You are already clean because of the word which I have spoken to you." (John 15:3).

Meaning, these readings are important because they are the words of the Lord Jesus Christ to us; they purify us and prepare us so we can complete the Liturgy pure by His word and be worthy to partake of His Pure Body and Precious Blood.

This is the importance of the Liturgy of the Word, which is crowned at its end by the Psalm and the Gospel. Meaning, all of this is not just a ritual and that's it; no, this is a life we live inside the church with the word of God, which ends in Communion. For my sake, focus on the words of the readings. Even if you read the Gospel at home, this is God's word to you and His voice to you alone, and His breath fills the corners of your home and your heart.

The Burial (Al-Dafna) & The Absolution of the Servants (Tahlil Al-Khudam)

I also spoke last time about the Burial (Al-Dafna), which is covering the Altar. The Prospharin symbolizes the stone, and the triangular Veil (Lafa'if) symbolizes the seals. All the servants of the Altar prostrate and exit the Sanctuary (Al-Haykal). Why do they exit? Because everyone went away and left Him alone.

The importance of this Absolution: Take note that Abouna who says the Absolution mentions his brother priest (or priests), the deacons, the clergy, and all the people. Pay attention to this point: it means you have received the Absolution. You are not coming as spectators, but as participants in the Liturgy. Because all the responses of the deacons outside the Sanctuary are actually your responses. In the Missal (Al-Khulagi), it is written "The People," not "The Deacons."

Abouna continues and says:

"From the mouth of the Holy Trinity, the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit; and from the mouth of the One, Only, Holy, Catholic, and Apostolic Church; and from the mouths of the Pure Apostles, and our Father Saint Mark the Beholder of God, the Evangelist."

Then he mentions the names of the saints who preserved the faith and protected the Church from heresies and false teachings. Then he mentions our Father the Patriarch Pope Tawadros, and our Father the Metropolitan Anba Serapion.

Then he says a phrase of utmost humility, even though this is his right, and part of his service, work, and responsibility in the Church—he has the right of binding and loosing for the salvation of every soul—when he says:

"and from the mouth of my abjection" (or "my weakness").

Even though he received this right from the Lord Jesus Christ Himself, when the Father Patriarch, Bishop, or Metropolitan breathed into his mouth to receive the Holy Spirit. Because this is the same breath that the Lord Jesus Christ breathed on the Apostles when He gave them the Mystery of Priesthood [2], and the one who handed down this breath in our Church is Saint Mark the Apostle.

After the Absolution (Tahlil):

Abouna enters the Sanctuary (Al-Haykal) and stands to the right of the Altar. The deacon presents the Censer (Shorya) to him, and he places five hands of incense in it, as I explained last time.

The beautiful thing in the Church is that as soon as Abouna holds the censer, we find the deacons outside the Sanctuary and the people chanting the hymn Tai Shori ("This is the Censer"). This beautiful hymn means:

"This is the Censer of Pure Gold, carrying the ember (Al-Anbar) [3], which is in the hands of Aaron the Priest, lifting up incense upon the Altar."

He circles around the Altar three times, goes out to finish his procession (Dora) in the nave of the church, and as he returns, it is called the Mystery of the Pauline Return (Sirr Raj'at Al-Bolis). In this, he gathers the prayers of the people and the papers from the people, and places all of this—requests and prayers—before God on the Altar in the secret prayer that Abouna says after he returns, which I explained last time.

(I also spoke with you about the 14 Epistles of Paul the Apostle last time).

The Catholic Epistle (Al-Catholicon)

Today, by the grace of God, I will speak with you about the second reading, which is the Catholicon (Al-Catholicon).

Meaning: The word "Catholicon" is a Greek word meaning "Universal" or "General." It means it was written for all believers throughout the inhabited world at that time, and for the Gentiles who believed in the Lord Jesus Christ and the Good News of the Gospel.

Notice here: Abouna does not offer incense, does not leave the Sanctuary (Al-Haykal), and there is no procession (Dora). He only reads the Mystery of the Catholicon while standing before the Altar.

What does this mean? It signifies the presence of the Apostles in Jerusalem and their not going out to preach the Gospel (the Good News) to the world during the period between the Ascension of our Savior, the Lord Jesus Christ, to the heavens until the descent of the Holy Spirit on the Day of Pentecost. As the Lord told them not to depart from Jerusalem but to wait for the Promise of the Father for the descent of the Holy Spirit upon them [4].

Abouna reads the Mystery of the Catholicon here. I will tell it to you so you can understand what is being said when Abouna is standing before the Altar inaudibly (Sirran):

"O Lord God, who through Your holy Apostles have revealed to us the mystery of the Gospel of the glory of Your Christ, and gave them the great and boundless gift of Your grace, that they should proclaim among all nations the unsearchable riches of Your mercy.

We ask You, O our Master, make us worthy of their portion and inheritance. Grant us at all times to walk in their footsteps, to imitate their struggle, and to share with them in the sweat (Al-A'raq) which they accepted for godliness. Guarding this Your Holy Church which You founded through them, and blessing the sheep of Your flock, and making to increase this vine which Your right hand has planted; through Christ Jesus our Lord, through Whom glory, honor, dominion, and worship are due to You, with Him and the Holy Spirit, the Giver of Life, who is of one essence with You, now and at all times and unto the ages of all ages. Amen."

(Note: "Unsearchable" means unlimited, endless. "Sweat" (Al-A'raq) means perspiration and toil).

So, what determines the readings in the Church?

A book called the Katamaros (Katamaros). The word Katamaros is a Greek word meaning "The Book of Readings" [5], and there are four books:

  1. Katamaros of the Days (Katamaros Al-Ayam): Determines the daily readings from Monday to Saturday throughout the year.
  2. Katamaros of the Sundays (Katamaros Al-Ahad): Determines the Sunday readings according to the Coptic months.
  3. Katamaros of Great Lent (Katamaros Al-Sawm Al-Kabir): Determines the readings according to the weeks of the Fast.
  4. Katamaros of the Holy Fifty Days (Katamaros Al-Khamasin): Determines the readings for every day until the Feast of Pentecost. (The Synaxarium is not read during the Holy Fifty Days because the Lord Jesus Christ is in the midst of the Church. The stories of the martyrs began appearing after the formation of the Church and the beginning of the eras of persecution).

Let's return to the Catholicon:

Before I begin, I must explain something important because there are two epistles under the names of James and Jude, and they are called "brothers of the Lord." I want to explain this to you so you can understand the genealogy and kinship, and not misunderstand it from any source that does not have historical evidence. (I remember explaining this part in the lecture on the Holy Family's Visit to Egypt).

According to the Bible Dictionary printed in London in 1863, Clopas was married to a woman also named Mary, whom the Scripture calls "the other Mary." She bore him four sons:

  1. James the Less (or James son of Alphaeus).
  2. Joses (or Joseph).
  3. Jude (surnamed Thaddeus).
  4. Simon.

Tradition says that Clopas was the brother of Joseph the Just (the Carpenter). Meaning, these are the cousins of the Lord Jesus Christ. In the East, they always call cousins "brothers"; they are considered like siblings.

The 7 Catholic Epistles:

  • The Epistle of Our Teacher Saint James, the brother of the Lord: He was the first Bishop of Jerusalem. It consists of 5 chapters. In it, he speaks about practical Christianity and Christian conduct led by the Holy Spirit. He addressed issues of testing faith, frankness, and controlling the tongue. He spoke about relying on God, the prayer of faith, and the work of healing through the prayer of faith. He taught the rich how to rely on God. He spoke about caring for orphans and widows as worship pleasing to God, and about avoiding partiality, especially in the Church. Meaning, the Epistle of our teacher James is a guide for those who desire Christian perfection in their practical life. He summarized all this when he said: "Faith without works is dead."
  • The First Epistle of Our Teacher Saint Peter: It consists of 5 chapters. In it, he encourages Christians, Jews, and Christians in the Diaspora who were suffering because of the persecution that happened after the martyrdom of Stephen, reminding them of the inheritance prepared for them in the Kingdom.
  • The Second Epistle of Our Teacher Saint Peter: It is 3 chapters. He warns believers against false teachers who distort the words of the Gospel. They must stand firm in the correct teaching and grow in the knowledge of Christ. He emphasizes that all words of the Holy Gospel are inspired by God, and that "no prophecy of Scripture is of any private interpretation, for prophecy never came by the will of man, but holy men of God spoke as they were moved by the Holy Spirit." He also spoke about what will happen in the End Times.
  • The First Epistle of Our Teacher Saint John: It is 5 chapters. All the epistles of our teacher John the Beloved revolve around love. That is why he focuses in his epistles on brotherly love. Until the last moment of his life, while they were carrying him on a stretcher to take him to church, he was speaking about love. The First Epistle revolves around our fellowship with God, how the Blood of Jesus Christ cleanses us from all sin, the atoning intercession of the Lord Jesus Christ, and His love for us in making us children of God.
  • The Second Epistle of Our Teacher John: It is 1 chapter. He wrote it to "the elect lady and her children" (Saint Kyria [6]), but it is written for each one of us. He speaks in it about Christian conduct in the Truth—and the Truth is the Lord Jesus Christ. The Way is the Lord Jesus Christ, because He said: "I am the Way, the Truth, and the Life." He urges us to walk in love according to the commandments of Christ, and warns against deceivers and false teachers, telling us not to share with them because they are against Christ.
  • The Third Epistle of Our Teacher Saint John: It is 1 chapter. He sent this to Gaius, his beloved brother in the Lord, who works for the Church, serving and receiving teachers and missionaries and caring for them. He encourages him in this, and also urges him to hold fast to the pure Christian life.
  • The Epistle of Our Teacher Saint Jude, the brother of the Lord: It is 1 chapter. He reminds the Church to be spiritually vigilant for the sake of the orthodox faith, and to be aware of the entry of false teachers who deny the existence of God. He gave examples of the destruction of the ungodly in the Old Testament, and the fall of Satan and his soldiers. The Church must stand firm on the faith handed down to her from the Apostles.

We notice something beautiful at the end of the reading of the Catholicon: The reader says:

"Do not love the world or the things in the world. The world is passing away, and the lust of it; but he who does the will of God abides forever. Amen."

This is taken from the First Epistle of our teacher John (1 John 2:15). It reminds us of the passing of the world and all its lusts. Who is the one who endures to the end? It is the one who keeps the commandments of Christ and does the will of God; he is the one who remains forever, meaning he wins the Kingdom of God. Because as our teacher John said: "For all that is in the world—the lust of the flesh, the lust of the eyes, and the pride of life—is not of the Father but is of the world." (1 John 2:16).

May God give us grace to live the moments of the Liturgy with all holiness, and fill our hearts with grace, peace, and brotherly love when Christ dwells with His Pure Body and Precious Blood inside us after Communion. To Him be the eternal glory forever. Amen.

Historical Information about the "Brothers of the Lord" (Sons of Alphaeus or Clopas):

Alphaeus (or Clopas): Tradition says he is the brother of Joseph the Just (the Guardian of the Incarnation). He was married to a woman named Mary, whom the Gospel mentions as "the other Mary" (and Tradition says she is the cousin of the Virgin Mary). She bore him four sons: James, Joses (or Joseph), Jude (surnamed Thaddeus), and Simon.

They used to resist the preaching of the Lord Jesus Christ, and they are the ones who tried to throw Him off the cliff. Tradition says that James fasted from Friday, the day of the Crucifixion, and said to himself that he would not taste food until the truth of the Resurrection appeared to him. Therefore, the Lord of Glory appeared to him specifically, broke bread for him, and fed him, raising him from disbelief and supporting his life with the Bread of Resurrection, the Bread of Eternal Life.

Our teacher Paul the Apostle clarified that the Lord of Glory appeared to James first [7] in his epistle to the Corinthians, saying: "After that He was seen by James, then by all the apostles." (1 Corinthians 15:7).

Saint James did not persecute the Church like Paul the Apostle, but rather resisted the Lord of the Church Himself during the days of His Incarnation. The Lord appeared specifically to James after His glorious Resurrection because his soul was zealous, and his asceticism and pure worship of the Law seemed precious in the eyes of the Lord. The Jews used to call him James the Just.

The power of the Resurrection changed James into a servant of Jesus Christ, as he began his epistle. The veil fell from his eyes, the news of his faith in Christ spread, and through him, a great and effective door was opened before the Church. For he is the "Brother of the Lord," the leader of the zealous, a pillar of faith and adherence, and he became the first Bishop of Jerusalem.
