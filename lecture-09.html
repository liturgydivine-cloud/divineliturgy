9 - Meet and Right

Summary:

Last time, we began explaining the first station in the Liturgy of the Faithful (Quddas Al-Mu'minin) or the Anaphora, which is the Prayer of Reconciliation (Salat Al-Solh). As I explained last time, the Liturgy of the Faithful consists of seven stations.

The Prayer of Reconciliation consists of two parts:

  1. A vertical part (from earth to Heaven): the reconciliation of earthly beings with heavenly beings through the Cross of the Lord Jesus Christ.
  2. A horizontal part: the reconciliation among everyone present in the church through the Holy Kiss (Al-Qubla Al-Muqaddasa), which takes place at the time the Prospharin (Al-Prospharin) is lifted.

The Prayer of Reconciliation begins with the first part, and Abouna's hands are bare, not covered by any veil (Lafa'if). This signifies the state of humanity, naked before God due to sin, until reconciliation was accomplished on the Cross.

  • The First Part of the Prayer of Reconciliation: Abouna prays to the Great and Eternal God who created man in incorruption, and continues that man fell through the envy of the devil and by not keeping God's commandment, until the Incarnation took place, and the praise of the angels: "Glory to God in the highest, and on earth peace," and He accomplished all of this on the Cross. After praying this, the deacon alerts the people to pray for perfect peace, love, and the pure apostolic kiss. This is so that everyone may examine themselves: Are they ready for this moment? Is there any grudge in their heart against anyone?
  • The Second Part of the Prayer of Reconciliation: Abouna takes the triangular veil, which represents the seal, and the deacon holds the Cross on the eastern side of the Altar. The veil demonstrates the blindness of the eyes to the salvation of Christ accomplished on the Cross, because sin blinds the eyes. As soon as Abouna removes the veil at the end of the prayer, he finds the Cross before his eyes, through which salvation was accomplished. That is why Abouna, in the second part, asks God to fill our hearts with His peace and cleanse us from all guile, blemish, hypocrisy, etc.

We also spoke about the lifting of the Prospharin (Al-Prospharin). It symbolizes the Resurrection of the Lord of Glory. As I said, this moment is awesome because it demonstrates the breaking and defeat of the devil before the Lord of Glory, Jesus Christ. I also explained the meaning of the Holy Kiss handed down by the Apostolic Fathers.

With this, we have finished the Prayer of Reconciliation, the first and important gate to continue the Liturgy, where you receive your "entry visa." Everyone must examine themselves to ensure their heart is pure, holding no grudge, and that the kiss is from the heart to everyone around them, not a deceitful kiss like the kiss of Judas. With this, we have passed the first "security checkpoint."

The Second Station: "It is Meet and Right" (Mostahaq wa Adel)

Today, by the grace of God, we will continue and explain the Second Station, which is the beginning of the prayers concerning the sanctification of the Body and Blood of the Lord. This is the station of "It is Meet and Right" (Mostahaq wa Adel).

This station draws us closer to the throne of God, describing this throne and everything around it. This prayer begins with a beautiful dialogue between Abouna and the people, so that we may be ready to pass through the second gate worthily. We say "It is meet and right" until we reach the Lord Jesus Christ on the throne, so we truly must be worthy of this encounter.

This prayer is a prayer of praise (Tasbeeh), because it teaches, clarifies, and reveals the love and glory of God in His wondrous work of creation. Praise is finding joy in God without asking anything from Him.

The dialogue begins when Abouna signs the people with the sign of the Cross and says:

"The Lord be with you all."

The people respond and say:

"And with your spirit."

Just as Abouna prays for the people, the people also pray for Abouna. As Paul the Apostle said: "You also helping together in prayer for us." (2 Corinthians 1:11).

This prayer contains two things:

  1. First, the priest asks God to be with all the people.
  2. Second, he alerts the people to the presence of God in their midst, so that everyone feels they are in the presence of God, performing their worship with all reverence and the awe of the Holy Spirit.

The most beautiful thing is when the people respond to the Father Priest and say, "And with your spirit"—mutual prayers!

Abouna signs the deacons with the sign of the Cross using the veil (Lafa'if) and says:

"Lift up your hearts," (or "Where are your hearts?").

The people answer:

"They are with the Lord."

Please, before you say "They are with the Lord," think for even one second: where is your heart really? Is it inside the church with you, or outside the church? Is your mind truly on God at this moment, and your heart in the fear of God? If you say "They are with the Lord" but your heart is somewhere else, then you are lying. How will you go in to take Communion? Forgive me for this word.

But at this moment, think and say: "O Lord, take my heart to Yourself, and my mind. Purify them and cleanse them. Fill my heart and mind with Your fear." (You must forget the world as soon as you enter the church door; your mind should be on Heaven and the Lord of Heaven). And then respond and say: "They are with the Lord."

Because here, Abouna alerts the people to lift up their hearts to God. To turn their minds away from the world and its preoccupations, and to focus their worship on the person of the Beloved Lord. Thus, everyone's focus will be on the things above, as our teacher Paul the Apostle says:

"If then you were raised with Christ, seek those things which are above, where Christ is, sitting at the right hand of God. Set your mind on things above, not on things on the earth." (Colossians 3:1-2).

Saint Cyril of Jerusalem said: "The priest commands us at that hour with authority to depart and leave all fleshly preoccupations, and set our minds in Heaven toward the Lover of Mankind." [1]

After that, Abouna signs himself with the sign of the Cross using the veil (Lafa'if) and prays, saying:

"Let us give thanks to the Lord."

The people respond and say:

"It is meet and right."

The priest thanks God because the people have lifted their hearts with full attention to God. When the people say "It is meet and right," this is the praise that the angels raise to God as they stand around the throne with ten thousands of saints. As mentioned in the Book of Revelation, when the angels praise the One sitting on the throne and say:

"Worthy is the Lamb who was slain to receive power and riches and wisdom, and strength and honor and glory and blessing!" (Revelation 5:12).

Also, the twenty-four elders sitting before God on their thrones:

"...fell on their faces and worshiped God, saying: 'We give You thanks, O Lord God Almighty...'" (Revelation 11:16-17).

The Prayer Continues: The Symphony of Praise

Abouna continues and says:

"Meet and right, meet and right, truly, indeed, it is meet and right."

He says it three times for emphasis. Meaning, Abouna takes what the people said—"meet and right"—and completes the prayer with it. This beautiful dialogue, which Abouna started in the first part of the prayer, is considered a beautiful symphony, a heavenly dialogue.

Because Abouna prays and contemplates:

"O God, who Is before the ages and reigns forever, who dwells in the highest and looks upon the lowly, who created the heaven, the earth, the sea, and all that is in them. The Father of our Lord, God, and Savior Jesus Christ, by Whom You have created all things, visible and invisible. Who sits upon the throne of His glory and is worshiped by all the holy powers."

This entire prayer is taken from the Psalms (31, 55, 15, 46, 113), from the Book of Genesis (1:1, 10), from 2 Corinthians, Ephesians 3, Colossians 1, Daniel 7, 1 Kings, Hebrews 1, and Romans 4.

You find Abouna reviewing the greatness of God the Creator, the Provider of His creation, and His providence in organizing the universe and the creatures in the world we see and the world we do not see. That is why John the Seer [2] said:

"Whenever the living creatures give glory and honor and thanks to Him who sits on the throne, who lives forever and ever..." (Revelation 4:9).

The Serving Deacon at the Altar alerts the people and says:

"You who are seated, stand."

Of course, this excludes the sick, the elderly, and those whose knees are worn out. But the purpose of this call for the people to stand is for their minds to be alert and attentive to worship, and for the people to participate with the heavenly beings and angels in standing before God. This reminds us of what John said in the Book of Revelation:

"All the angels stood around the throne..." (Revelation 7:11).

The Second Part of the Prayer "It is Meet and Right"

Abouna continues and says:

"You before Whom stand the Angels, the Archangels, the Principalities, the Authorities, the Thrones, the Dominions, and the Powers."

Here Abouna mentioned 7 of the heavenly ranks standing before the throne of God [3]. The ranks standing before God are supposed to be nine orders (Taghmat). Abouna completes them in the third part of the prayer "It is Meet and Right," as we will see.

The Serving Deacon in the Sanctuary calls out and says:

"Look towards the East."

This is an apostolic tradition (Tasleem Rasouli), which Pope Athanasius the Apostolic interpreted for three reasons:

  1. The Garden of Eden, from which we were exiled, was in the East. (Genesis 2:8).
  2. The Lord, the Sun of Righteousness, came from the East. (Matthew 2:2).
  3. The Lord Jesus Christ will come again in the Second Coming from the East. (Matthew 24:27, Zechariah 14:4, Acts 1:11).

And His Holiness Pope Shenouda III added and said: 4. The Lord Jesus Christ rose from the dead and met with His disciples on the Mount of Olives facing the East. 5. He also said that Isaiah the Prophet stated: "Therefore glorify the Lord in the dawning light [the East]." (Isaiah 24:15).

The Third Part of the Prayer "It is Meet and Right"

Abouna prays and says:

"You around Whom stand the Cherubim full of eyes, and the Seraphim with six wings, praising continuously without ceasing, saying..."

Here, the people pray, saying:

"The Cherubim worship You, and the Seraphim glorify You, proclaiming and saying: Holy, Holy, Holy, Lord of Sabaoth (Sabaoth); heaven and earth are full of Your holy glory." (Isaiah 6:3).

The word Sabaoth is a Hebrew word meaning "hosts" or the angels who are the armies of heaven [4].

With this, Abouna has completed the heavenly ranks (Taghmat), bringing them to nine ranks. However, the number is still incomplete before God, who is perfect in everything. Therefore, He created man in His image and likeness so that, if he kept the commandment, he would fill the rank left vacant by the fall of Satan, and man would become among the heavenly beings to complete their number before God's absolute perfection [5].

Man (Adam) did not keep the commandment and fell. But God arranged salvation, bore the sins of the world, and opened Paradise once again through the Cross. He restored Adam to his former rank in a better and far superior state, because in the Second Coming, everyone's earthly bodies will be transformed into luminous, spiritual bodies like the angels. In this way, the number of ranks before the throne of God will be complete: ten ranks. May God grant us a portion among them.

This Praise: "Holy, Holy, Holy, Lord of Sabaoth"

Isaiah the Prophet heard it from the mouths of the angels in his vision, when he saw God sitting in His holy temple (Isaiah 6:3). We chant it to participate with the angels in Heaven in praising God.

Saint Gregory said: "This praise is chanted to signify reconciliation and union—the union of angels with humans and their agreement in praise, and to signify that through Christ Jesus they have become one Church." [6]

With this, we have finished the Second Station of the Anaphora. Next time, if the Lord gives us life and we live, we will complete the Third Station, which is the Prayer of Agios (Salat Agios). To our God be all glory and honor forever. Amen.

[2] John the Seer: Title for St. John the Apostle referring to his authorship of the Book of Revelation (the Visionary). ↩

[5] Tenth Rank: Traditional teaching that humanity was created to replace the fallen angels, becoming the tenth rank around the throne. ↩
