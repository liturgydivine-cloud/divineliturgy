The Prayer of Agios

Summary of the Previous Lesson:

We covered the station of "It is Meet and Right" (Mostahaq wa Adel). This is the prayer that features a beautiful dialogue between Abouna and the people, and it prepares us so that we may be worthy to reach the throne of the Lord Jesus Christ at the end of the Liturgy.

  • Abouna signs the people with the sign of the Cross and says: "The Lord be with you all."
  • The people respond and say: "And with your spirit." (Meaning, it is a mutual prayer between the people and Abouna, and Abouna also wants to alert the people that they are in the presence of God).
  • Then Abouna signs the deacons with the sign of the Cross and says: "Lift up your hearts" (or "Where are your hearts?").
  • The people respond and say: "They are with the Lord."

I explained last time that we must think before we respond, ensuring that my heart is truly inside the church, not outside the church. I should be thinking about the words of the Liturgy so my response is not false or just a routine. As Saint Cyril of Jerusalem said:

"The priest commands us at that hour with authority to depart and leave all fleshly preoccupations, and set our minds in Heaven toward the Lover of Mankind."
  • The Third Sign: Abouna signs himself with the sign of the Cross using the Veil (Lafa'if) and says: "Let us give thanks to the Lord."
  • Here, the people respond and say: "It is meet and right."

When the people truly say this phrase from their hearts—that God is meet and right—Abouna knows that the people have lifted their hearts to God, because this is a miniature picture of Heaven. The angels before the throne, as the Book of Revelation describes the One sitting on the throne:

"Worthy is the Lamb who was slain to receive power and riches and wisdom, and strength and honor and glory and blessing!"

The Book of Revelation also describes the 24 elders sitting before God on their thrones, falling down on their faces in worship and saying: "We give You thanks, O Lord God Almighty." Notice in this prayer that Abouna reviews the greatness of God, the Creator, the Provider, and the Organizer of the entire universe.

The call of the deacon, saying: "You who are seated, stand." In reality, we are supposed to stand throughout the Liturgy. There is no sitting; no one should sit except the sick, the elderly, and those who have problems with their legs. In Russia, the churches have no pews; there are only benches around the walls of the church for the elderly and the sick to sit on [1]. Why this call? So that everyone is alert and attentive to worship.

The Second Part of "It is Meet and Right": Abouna takes us to the place where the angels stand. We must imagine the beautiful scene, a miniature picture of Heaven. Abouna reminds us of the orders (Taghmat) or ranks of angels: 1. Angels, 2. Archangels, 3. Principalities, 4. Authorities, 5. Thrones, 6. Dominions, 7. Powers.

The Third Part of "It is Meet and Right": Abouna continues and leads us into the circle of the Cherubim and Seraphim who praise God without ceasing, saying:

"Holy, holy, holy, Lord of Sabaoth, heaven and earth are full of Your glory and Your honor." (Isaiah 6:3).

The Prayer of Agios (Salat Agios)

Abouna begins to pray and say, "Holy, holy, holy," and takes us to the Third Station of the Divine Liturgy, which is the Prayer of Agios.

The Prayer of Agios (Holy) begins after the people finish the praise of the Cherubim and Seraphim. What happens is that Abouna begins to do something you might not see clearly because his back is to you; those who see it clearly are the servants of the Sanctuary (Al-Haykal).

What happens is that Abouna begins to change the position of the Veils (Lafa'if) while the people are saying "Holy, holy, holy." He takes the veil that is on his left hand and moves it to the right side, and the veil that is on his right hand he moves to the left side. Then, he removes the veil that is on the Chalice (Kass) and places it on his right hand, and takes the veil he moved from the left side and places it on the Chalice, covering the Chalice with it [2].

What is the meaning of this?

  • Our sins have been covered and forgiven by the Blood of Christ when He bore all our sins upon Himself on the Cross, and He transferred us from the "left" order to the "right" order.
  • The veil that was on the Chalice is a symbol of the righteousness of the Lord Jesus Christ, which clothed us and enveloped us with His righteousness when He bore our sins.
  • Placing the veil that was on the left hand onto the Chalice has a symbolic meaning, indicating that this Body is for this Blood, and this Blood is for this Body [3].

The movement of the veils in this manner, transferring and arranging them in this way, reminds us of the Resurrection of our Lord Jesus Christ, and how John found the linen cloths lying there, and the handkerchief that had been around the Savior's head folded together in a place by itself (John 20:6-7). The movement of the veils, their transfer, and then their arrangement in this manner reminds us of the Resurrection of our Lord Jesus Christ. It was a pure, blessed movement, not an act of theft as the Jews claimed, because everything was left perfectly in order in the tomb.

The First Part of the Prayer of Agios (Salat Agios):

Abouna holds the Cross with the veil (Lafa'if) he took from the Chalice and makes three signs (Rushumat) while saying "Agios" or "Ekouab." Both mean the same thing: the first is in Greek, and the second is in Coptic.

  1. The First Sign: Over himself (Abouna signs himself).
  2. The Second Sign: Over the servants on his right.
  3. The Third Sign: Over the people.

(If the Father Metropolitan or Bishop is present, he is the one who says Agios three times with the signs).

The Three Signs that Abouna, the Bishop, or the Metropolitan makes over himself, the deacons, and the people are a sign of God's grace, which, through the death and resurrection of the Lord Jesus Christ, was poured out and encompassed all classes of humanity.

After that, as you know, the Paten (Siniya) represents the Manger or the Tomb. After transferring the veils, the Paten is uncovered, but the Chalice remains covered. There is a beautiful meaning here:

  • The covered Chalice: Symbolizes the appearance of the Lord Jesus Christ to Mary Magdalene while hiding His identity from her initially.
  • The uncovered Paten: Means that when the women came to the tomb, they found the stone rolled away—which is the veil that was on the Paten.

From all this, we come away with a beautiful understanding: that God has granted us the knowledge of the mysteries of the Kingdom of Heaven.

The beginning of the prayer is beautiful: When Abouna says:

"Holy, Holy, Holy, indeed, O Lord our God,"

he begins to tell the story of creation. How He prepared for us the Paradise of Joy; how, when He commanded us, we broke His commandment through the deception of the serpent, and were exiled from the Paradise of Joy. Yet, He did not forget us or leave us, but visited us through His holy prophets. And out of His love for us, in the last days, He appeared to us—we poor ones sitting in darkness and the shadow of death—through His Only-Begotten Son, our Lord, God, and Savior Jesus Christ.

Here the people cry out and say: "Amen." How? Because: "He was incarnate of the Holy Spirit and of the Virgin Mary."

Pay attention here: When Abouna says this phrase, the Censer (Shorya) is next to his hand, presented by the deacon. The censer is the symbol of the Virgin who carried the ember of Divinity. He places a hand of incense and says: "He was incarnate and became man, and taught us the paths of salvation."

Do you see the beauty of the Liturgy? The symbol of the Virgin is the censer, the incense is the symbol of Christ's priesthood, and the burning coal represents the Divinity of Christ in the womb of the Virgin [4]. Both together form a living movement that expresses the words and the prayer in the censer presented by the deacon to Abouna.

Abouna places the hand of incense, saying:

"He was incarnate and became man, and taught us the paths of salvation."

The incense symbolizes the priesthood of the Lord Jesus Christ. It also points to the works of the Lord Jesus Christ in His incarnation for our salvation: He offered Himself as a ransom for us, an offering and a sweet-smelling sacrifice—a sacrifice of joy on Golgotha. As our teacher Paul the Apostle said: "And walk in love, as Christ also has loved us and given Himself for us, an offering and a sacrifice to God for a sweet-smelling aroma." (Ephesians 5:2).

The Second Part of the Prayer of Agios (Salat Quddus):

After placing the hand of incense, Abouna begins to pray, saying:

"He was incarnate and became man, and taught us the paths of salvation. He granted us the birth from on high through water and Spirit (which is Baptism, the new birth). He made us unto Himself a congregated people, and sanctified us by Your Holy Spirit. He who loved His own who were in the world, and gave Himself up as a ransom on our behalf unto death which reigned over us..."

He continues the prayer until he reaches: "He descended into Hades through the Cross." Abouna summarizes the story of salvation: Why did God do this? Out of His love for the world, for the human race, and He redeemed us from death on the Cross. Here, the people respond and confirm Abouna's words, saying: "We believe it is true." (Haqqan Nu'min).

I wish this word would come from the heart, and we would say to Him: "O Lord, we thank You for all the salvation You have prepared for us since the fall of our father Adam, and for Your love for us and Your sufferings for my sake."

The Third Part of the Prayer:

Abouna begins and says in it:

"He rose from the dead on the third day, ascended into the heavens, and sat at Your right hand, O Father. He appointed a day for retribution, on which He will appear to judge the world in righteousness and give to each one according to his deeds."

Abouna reminds us in this prayer of the Resurrection of the Lord of Glory from the dead on the third day, His ascension to the heavens, and His sitting—meaning stability in the power and greatness of the Divine Being—at the right hand of the Father, indicating the power of God. He appointed a day for retribution, to appear to judge the entire inhabited world in righteousness and give to each one according to his works and deeds.

That's it; the time of mercy has ended. The door of mercy is closed. The door of grace is closed. The awesome Day of Judgment will come. Here, the people, the deacons, and Abouna—meaning everyone present in the church—feel this awesome moment, and the people cry out, saying:

"According to Your mercy, O Lord, and not according to our sins."

This is accompanied by beating the chest. It is not just a ritualistic movement; no, it is true repentance, a brokenness of heart asking for God's mercies. Because truly, if God treated us according to our sins, we would all perish. "They have all turned aside; they have together become corrupt... and fall short of the glory of God." (Romans 3:12, 23). That is why there is no refuge except one: the love and mercy of God. That is why we say, "According to Your mercy, O Lord," from the depth of the heart.

Placing the Veils & Incensing the Hands

Notice here that Abouna places the two veils (Lafa'if) from his hands next to the Throne of the Chalice (Kursi Al-Kass) on the right and the left. He points to the bread and the wine and says: "He instituted for us this great mystery of godliness."

Placing the two veils next to the throne has a beautiful meaning: It represents the occurrence of a calm, organized movement in the tomb, indicating life and Resurrection. It also reminds us of Peter and John's inspection and search of the tomb, and contemplating the veils and linen cloths they found lying in the tomb. But the handkerchief that had been around the head of the Lord Jesus Christ was by itself.

(According to Jewish tradition, when a servant prepares a meal for the master of the house, the napkin and the utensils he will eat with are folded and placed next to the food. If the master gets up from the meal for any reason, he folds the napkin and leaves it. This means he is coming back to finish eating. But if he leaves the napkin unfolded anywhere, it means he has finished dinner, and the servants can clear all the food from the table. The folded handkerchief in the tomb means that He will come a second time for judgment). [5]

While the people are saying: "According to Your mercy, O Lord, and not according to our sins," the deacon presents the Censer (Shorya) to him. Abouna incenses his hands three times, takes some of the incense smoke, and places it over the Body and the Chalice while saying:

"He instituted for us this great mystery of godliness."

Abouna incenses his hands in preparation to touch the Lamb placed in the Paten (Siniya), which he will hold in his hands afterward. Placing the incense over the Oblation (Qurban) and the Chalice symbolizes the spices (Hanoot) that Joseph of Arimathea and Nicodemus placed on the body of the Savior when they buried Him, and the spices that the women brought to the tomb to anoint the body of the Savior.

After that, Abouna lifts the Star / Dome (Al-Nigma / Al-Qubba) from the Paten. This represents the star that guided the Magi to the place of the Lord Jesus Christ, and when they found Him, the star disappeared from them. It is also like the angels who announced the good news to the shepherds and disappeared after making the place of birth known to them [6]. Furthermore, lifting the Star is so that the face of the Lord may be seen without a barrier, not like Moses when the Lord spoke to him from behind the cloud.

Abouna continues and says: "For when He was determined to give Himself up to death for the life of the world..." The people respond and say: "We believe."

Our teacher Paul the Apostle explained this point in 1 Corinthians chapter 11. He points to the love of God that the Lord Jesus declared to us on that night in which He was betrayed. In it, He instituted this great Mystery and handed it to His disciples. To signify the greatness of this divine love, the Apostle Paul specified the time by saying "on the same night." It is as if he is saying: On that night when people were preoccupied with handing the Son of God over to death, the Lord Jesus was preoccupied with giving eternal life to the world. And while people were preoccupied with preparing the Cross and the tomb for Him, He was preparing for them the divine banquet and the table of salvation.

"That the Lord Jesus on the same night in which He was betrayed took bread; and when He had given thanks, He broke it and said, 'Take, eat; this is My body which is broken for you; do this in remembrance of Me.'" (1 Corinthians 11:23-24).

After that, Abouna lifts the Lamb with his right hand, kisses it, and transfers it to his left hand. There is a Veil (Lafa'if) underneath the Lamb, which he removes, kisses, and places on the Altar [7] while saying:

"He took bread upon His holy, immaculate, unblemished, blessed, and life-giving hands."

The people respond and say: "We believe that this is true, Amen."

We will continue next week because this topic has a lot of explanation. To our God be all glory and honor forever. Amen.

[1] Standing in Liturgy: Standard posture for prayer in early tradition, symbolizing reverence and readiness before the King. ↩
[2] Switching the Veils: Deeply symbolic ritual visually affirming that Christ transferred us from sin (left) to righteousness (right). ↩
[3] Body is for Blood: Affirms the unity of the sacrament; Christ is entirely present in both the Bread and the Wine. ↩
[4] The Censer (Shorya): Marian symbol where the bowl is her womb, the coal is Christ's humanity, and the fire is His divinity. ↩
[5] The Folded Handkerchief: Traditional explanation indicating that since the tomb was left in order, He intends to return. ↩
[6] The Star (Al-Nigma): Metal arches over the Paten. Removing it symbolizes that the Star of Bethlehem has led us to the reality of the Sacrifice. ↩
[7] Removing the Veil: Removing the small veil under the Lamb signifies that Christ is no longer hidden but is now openly offered. ↩
