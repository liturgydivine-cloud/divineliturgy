The Litanies

Summary of the Previous Lesson:

Last time, I spoke with you about the Prayer of Agios (Salat Agios), which is the heart of the Divine Liturgy (Al-Quddas Al-Ilahi), the signs (Rushumat) over the Lamb, and the signs over the Chalice (Kass) with the sign of the Holy Cross, which is the seal of the Lord Jesus Christ, three times. Abouna says: "Gave thanks, blessed it, and sanctified it." And each time, the people pray and say: "Amen."

I spoke about dividing the Oblation (Qurbana) into one-third on the right (the side of the three holes) and two-thirds on the left, such that this division does not affect the Spadikon (Al-Isbadikon). The division is from top to bottom without separating it completely, and Abouna divides a part of the Lamb above the Spadikon and from below as well.

We learned the meaning of dividing the Lamb: the one-third symbolizes the Son, and the two-thirds symbolize the Father and the Holy Spirit. The Son incarnated, suffered, died, and rose, but He did not separate from the Father and the Holy Spirit. The inheritance is steadfast in Heaven and does not change; the inheritance of the Son sitting at the right hand of Majesty is one inheritance, which we also inherited with Him.

We also learned that moving the Chalice in the shape of a cross means that the Lord Jesus Christ shed His blood on the Cross for the salvation of the whole world, because the Chalice moved in the four directions of the entire inhabited world. And when the people say: "Amen, Amen, Amen, Your death, O Lord, we proclaim... etc." From here begins the point of transformation (Al-Tahawwul).

We find Abouna saying: "Now, therefore, as we also commemorate His holy passion... etc." Abouna prostrates (Yasjud) in reverence and asks for the Holy Spirit. He prays for the Holy Spirit to descend upon the Oblations (Al-Qarabeen) and transform them, and he also asks that the Holy Spirit descend upon everyone present in the church as well. This is the Prayer of Invocation (Epiclesis).

At this moment, the Serving Deacon says: "Worship God in fear and trembling." Everyone prostrates quietly. That is why the deacon says: "Let us attend" (Nonsit).

Abouna rises from prostration and quickly signs the Lamb with three signs, saying:

"And this Bread, He makes it a Holy Body of His."

(The people respond: "I believe, Amen.") Abouna prostrates again and says inaudibly (Sirran): "Our Lord, God, and Savior Jesus Christ, given for the remission of sins and eternal life to those who partake of Him." Abouna rises again quickly and signs the Chalice with three signs, saying: "And this Cup also, a Precious Blood of His New Covenant." (He prostrates again, and the people say: "We also believe.")

After these signs, Abouna stands and says: "Our Lord, God, and Savior Jesus Christ, given for the remission of sins and eternal life to those who partake of Him"—just as he said after the signs over the Body. The people and the deacons stand, saying "Lord have mercy" (Kyrie Eleison) three times, asking for mercy from Emmanuel present on the Altar.

At the end of this summary, I want to clarify something important for those who say that the Liturgy is merely a "memorial" of the Incarnation, Passion, Crucifixion, and Resurrection of the Lord Jesus Christ. If that were truly the case and reality, we would be the most miserable and unhappy of all people [1].

But the Liturgy is an actual, extended reality of an event that happened in the past but is still ongoing and standing by the power of God and His Holy Spirit. It is an extension of an event that occurred in the past but continues. The most accurate word to clarify this meaning is the Greek word Anamnesis (Anamnesis), which means bringing a past event into the present before God. (From the book "The Divine Liturgy: The Mystery of the Kingdom of God," Part 2, by Abouna Athanasius El-Makary).

The Fourth Station of the Divine Liturgy: The Litanies (Al-Awashi) or Supplications

After the end of the Third Station ("Agios"), we now have on the Holy Altar (Al-Mazbah) or the Holy Table a true Body and Blood of our God and Savior Jesus Christ, present with us. That is why we find Abouna beginning to pray and present requests to the Lord of Glory for the sake of every person and for the sake of the world. These holy requests are called Litanies (Oushias / Prayers).

Before Abouna begins the Litanies, we find him covering his hands with the Veils (Lafa'if) because he is standing before the Body and Blood of the Lord Jesus Christ, just as the angels do: with two wings they cover their faces, with two they cover their feet, and with two they fly [2]. (This is an opportunity for you to pray and ask God for ourselves, for others, and for every person dear to you).

Before Abouna prays the Litanies, he prays a beautiful introduction when he says:

"Make us all worthy, O our Master, to partake of Your Holies unto the purification of our souls, our bodies, and our spirits. That we may become one body and one spirit, and may have a share and an inheritance with all the saints who have pleased You since the beginning."

After that, he begins to pray the Seven Litanies. This beautiful prayer shows what happens within us when we are worthy to partake of the Body and Blood of our Lord Jesus Christ:

  1. First, He grants and gives purification to our souls.
  2. Second, to our bodies.
  3. Third, to our spirits.

What else does He do in us if we partake worthily? He makes us one body, one spirit, and one mind. How "one mind"? Is not the Lord Jesus Christ the Head of the Church? And what is the Church? It is the congregation of believers. Meaning, we have the mind of Christ. And the last thing we receive is to find a share and an inheritance with all the saints who have pleased the Lord and lived a life of holiness (which is the Victorious Church). This means we must struggle in prayer and ask for help from the Lord of the Sacrifice so that we may have a share with the saints who truly lived a life of holiness and pleased the Lord with their good deeds while they were on earth.

1. The First Litany (Oushia): The Litany of Peace

Abouna prays and says:

"Remember, O Lord, the peace of Your One, Only, Holy, Catholic, and Apostolic Church."

How was the Church formed, and how did it come to be? Through the Blood of the Lord Jesus Christ which He shed on the Cross, and the Blood and Water that came out from His side. (This point is clarified when Abouna says, "This which You have acquired to Yourself with the precious Blood of Your Christ," while pointing to the Chalice and the Body).

It was founded and born on the Day of Pentecost by the descent of the Holy Spirit on the pure Disciples and Apostles. The word "acquired" (Iqtanaytaha) means "owned" or "kept for Yourself until now." ("And the gates of Hades shall not prevail against it," this is our Lord's promise to us) [3]. And He protects it from the snares of the devil and his soldiers. It is "One and Only," as the Holy Spirit described it in the Song of Solomon: "My dove, my perfect one, is the only one, the only one of her mother." (Song of Solomon 6:9). And our teacher Paul the Apostle described it as Holy, Catholic, and Apostolic.

2. The Second Litany (Oushia): The Litany of the Fathers

Reverend Abouna begins it after he says:

"This which You have acquired to Yourself with the precious Blood of Your Christ, keep her in peace, with all the orthodox bishops who are in her."

He begins by mentioning first our Father the Patriarch, Anba Tawadros, the Archbishop. He also mentions his spiritual brothers, the Patriarch of Antioch and the Patriarch of Eritrea [4]. He also mentions his partner in the apostolic ministry, the Metropolitan or the Bishop of the Diocese in which the church is located. These are the shepherds responsible for the churches in their countries, who watch over their flock through their prayers and supplications.

Of course, the response of the people in all the Litanies is: "Kyrie Eleison, Lord have mercy." At the same time, we are supposed to be praying for our Father the Patriarch, saying: "O Lord, keep him for us and preserve him for many years and peaceful times. O Lord, support him, help him, give him wisdom..."

Over the years, the Popes of Alexandria have stood against heresies and false teachings. They were the defenders of the faith and the "Judges of the Universe" [5], as the history books say throughout the years.

3. The Third Litany (Oushia): The Litany of the Priests

He prays and says:

"And those who righty divide the word of truth with him, grant them to Your Holy Church to shepherd Your flock in peace. Remember, O Lord, the hegoumens, the priests, and the deacons."

These priestly ranks help the Bishop in rightly dividing the word of truth. The word "dividing" (Tafsil) means explaining the word and the verse accurately, without adding or subtracting, or adding things that harm the meaning and lead to a false understanding or heresy [6]. They also help the Bishop or Metropolitan in pastoring the people, visiting them, and caring for them for the salvation of their souls.

4. The Fourth Litany (Oushia): The Litany of Mercy (or The Servants)

Abouna prays and says:

"And all the ministers, and all who are in virginity, and the purity of all Your faithful people. Remember, O Lord, to have mercy upon us all together."

Who are the servants (Khuddam)? Sunday School servants, the servants who bake the Oblation (Qurban), the servants who clean the church, those responsible for visiting the sick and needy families. Any service in the church, whatever it may be, has its value before our Lord.

And those who are in virginity (Batouliya): We all know what virginity is, like the monks and nuns, and there are also celibate laypeople living in the world who have dedicated themselves to the service of Christ.

Abouna completes the picture when he says, "And the purity of all Your faithful people." At the end of this Litany, Abouna gathers it all and says: "Remember, O Lord, to have mercy upon us all together." The reaction of the people at this moment is to cry out and say: "Have mercy upon us, O God the Father the Pantocrator." [7] Oh, how wonderful this response is if it comes from the depth of our hearts!

5. Fifth Litany (Oushia): The Litany of the Place

In this Litany, Abouna prays and says:

"Remember, O Lord, the salvation of this Your holy place, and all places, and all the monasteries of our Orthodox fathers."

"This place" refers to the church we are praying in. What clarifies this Litany is the response of the Serving Deacon when he says: "Pray for the salvation of the world, and of this our city, and all cities, districts, islands, and monasteries." [8] Because the salvation and peace of the world reflect on the children of the Church.

6. Sixth Litany (Oushia): The Litany of the Waters, Crops, or Air and Fruits

Of course, this applies to Egypt because there are three seasons (Waters, Crops, and Harvest). However, the Holy Synod combined these three litanies into one litany for the churches in the diaspora, and it is prayed throughout the entire year [9]. Abouna prays and says:

"Graciously accord, O Lord, to bless the air of heaven, the fruits of the earth, the waters of the rivers, the seeds, the herbs, and the plants of the field this year."

This is the only litany where the people respond with "Lord have mercy" (Kyrie Eleison) three times. Because everything mentioned in it is the core of life for all creation.

7. The Seventh Litany (Oushia): The Litany of the Oblations (Al-Qarabeen)

However, Abouna does not pray it until after this beautiful prayer he offers to Emmanuel, present with us on the Holy Altar:

"Bring them up according to their measure, according to Your grace. Give joy to the face of the earth; may its furrows be abundantly watered, and its fruits be multiplied."

The first part of this prayer shows the contentment of the Christian person in the phrase "according to their measure" (Ka-Miqdariha), meaning just enough. Abouna continues and says: "Prepare it for sowing and harvesting. Direct our lives as deemed fit." You are the Provider of this universe; therefore, direct our lives as deemed fit. As our teacher Paul the Apostle said: "And having food and clothing, with these we shall be content."

After that, Abouna continues and says: "Bless the crown of the year with Your goodness, for the sake of the poor of Your people, the widow, the orphan, the stranger, and the guest; and for the sake of us all who entreat You and seek Your Holy Name." Reverend Abouna mentions before Him the poor, the widow, the orphan, the stranger, and the guest—he leaves no one out.

Abouna continues and says: "For the eyes of everyone wait upon You, for You give them their food in due season." We always look with the boldness of children (Dallat Al-Banin) toward their Heavenly Father, confident that You do not forget them.

The last section of this prayer is the peak of beauty and complete surrender to God when Abouna says:

"Deal with us according to Your goodness, O You who give food to all flesh. Fill our hearts with joy and gladness, that we too, having sufficiency in every thing always, may abound in every good deed." [10]

Whatever You see as good, O Lord, do it for us, because the bitterness You choose for us is better than the honey we choose for ourselves. In the end: "Fill our hearts with joy and gladness." Because a joyful heart feels safe and at peace. That is why we say to You, "Give us this day our daily bread," so we may be a source of blessing to others.

After this beautiful prayer, Abouna prays the Litany of the Oblations (Oushiat Al-Qarabeen):

"Remember, O Lord, those who have brought unto You these gifts, those on whose behalf they have been brought, and those by whom they have been brought. Give them all the heavenly reward."

Every offering the believers bring (Tithes, flour for the Qurban, Abaraka, candles, altar vessels, etc.) are considered offerings. During the prayer of this Litany, Abouna points with his hands to the Mysteries present before him, because they are the peak of all offerings. They want an eternal heavenly reward, not a fleeting earthly reward.

And to our God be all glory and honor forever. Amen.

[2] Covering the Hands: The priest covers his hands with veils out of reverence for the consecrated Eucharist, mirroring the Seraphim in Isaiah 6:2. ↩
[3] "Gates of Hades": Reference to Matthew 16:18, emphasizing the indestructibility of the Church. ↩
[4] Sister Churches: Oriental Orthodox Churches share full communion and commemorate each other's Patriarchs. ↩
[5] Judge of the Universe: Historical title for the Pope of Alexandria, who calculated the date of Easter for all Christendom. ↩
[6] Rightly Dividing the Word of Truth: Based on 2 Timothy 2:15, meaning to teach the Word of God accurately. ↩
[7] The Cry for Mercy: A central moment where the whole Church relies entirely on God's mercy. ↩
[8] Islands (Jaza'ir): Ancient liturgical term reflecting the early Christian world around the Mediterranean. ↩
[9] Combined Litany: The Holy Synod combined the seasonal Egyptian agricultural prayers for use in the diaspora throughout the year. ↩
[10] "Having sufficiency...": A quote from 2 Corinthians 9:8, highlighting that God provides so we can be generous. ↩
