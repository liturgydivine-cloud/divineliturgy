The Institution Narrative

Summary of the Previous Lesson:

During the prayer of the praise of the Cherubim and Seraphim, Abouna changes the position of the Veils (Lafa'if), as I explained last time, and we learned the spiritual meaning of changing the veils:

  • Placing the veil that was on his left hand onto the Chalice means our sins were covered and forgiven by the Blood of Christ on the Cross. He bore all our sins and transferred us from the "left" order to the "right" order.
  • Placing the veil on the Chalice is a symbol and indication that this Body is for this Blood, and this Blood is for this Body.
  • The veil that was on the Chalice is a symbol of the righteousness of Christ, which clothed us, enveloped us with His righteousness, and bore our sins; He was stripped naked in order to cover our sins.
  • The movement of the veils in this manner, transferring and arranging them in this shape, reminds us of the Resurrection of our Lord Jesus Christ, and how Saint John found the linen cloths lying there, and the handkerchief that had been around the head of the Lord Jesus Christ folded together in a place by itself.

We covered the First Part of the Prayer of Agios (Holy). It contains three signs (Rushumat): Abouna signs himself, the second sign is for the servants on his right, and the third sign is for the people. This means that the death and resurrection of the Lord Jesus Christ has encompassed all humanity.

I explained the meaning of uncovering the Paten (Siniya) and covering the Chalice (Kass). I explained the prayers prayed by Reverend Abouna, which begin with "Holy, holy, holy."

I explained the Second Part of the prayer, when Abouna places a hand of incense in the Censer (Shorya) while saying "He was incarnate of the Holy Spirit and of the Virgin Mary," and the deacon presents the censer to him, and he places the hand of incense in it. I explained the beautiful meaning of this scene.

The Third Part of the Prayer of Agios: In it, Abouna prays about the Lord's Resurrection from the dead, His Ascension into the heavens, and His sitting in the power and greatness of the Divine Being. The "right hand of the Father" refers to the power of God, and that He appointed a day for retribution. The people respond and say: "According to Your mercy, O Lord, and not according to our sins."

The deacon presents the censer to Abouna to incense and purify his hands. At the same time, he takes some of the incense smoke and places it over the Oblation (Qurban) and the Chalice. This symbolizes the spices (Hanoot) that Joseph of Arimathea and Nicodemus placed on the body of the Lord, and also the spices that the women brought to the tomb.

I spoke about a beautiful contemplation on the night the Mystery of the Eucharist (Eucharistia) was instituted. Its summary is that while the Lord of Glory was preparing eternal life, the divine banquet, and the table of salvation for the people, they were preparing for Him death and crucifixion.

The Institution Narrative (The Words of Institution)

Today we continue the Prayer of Agios, when Abouna lifts the Lamb from the Paten (Siniya) with his right hand and places it on his left hand. He says:

"He took bread upon His holy, immaculate, unblemished, blessed, and life-giving hands."

The people respond and say: "We believe that this is true, Amen."

Saint Jacob of Edessa [1] explained these words when he said:

"When the Lord Jesus Christ ate the legal lamb (a symbol of the Lord Jesus Christ) with His holy disciples, which represents the heavenly Lamb, He desired to free us from the sacrifices of sheep, goats, and bulls, and elevate us to the sublimity of the heavenly sacrifices and the theological mysteries; so He took bread in His hands."

When Abouna lifts the Lamb in his hand, this signifies that the Body of the Lord Jesus Christ was lifted up on the Cross. And when he says, "upon His holy, immaculate, unblemished hands," it reminds us here of the hands that performed miracles with the blind, the sick, and the raising of the dead. Meaning, the hands that did all good for humanity and provided all gifts to the needy. Here, the people respond and confirm these words, saying: "We believe that this is true, Amen."

Of course, as soon as Abouna holds the Lamb in his hand, the deacons serving the Sanctuary light the candles to illuminate the Lamb and the Chalice. Because this moment in the Liturgy is an awesome moment, and the candles indicate the gravity and importance of this moment.

Abouna places the index finger of his right hand on the Lamb while saying:

"He looked up towards heaven to You, O God, who are His Father and Master of everyone." [2]

Before any momentous work, the Lord Jesus Christ would lift His eyes to heaven. For example, at the raising of Lazarus, the miracle of the five loaves and two fish, and His prayer before the Cross in the Gospel of John, Chapter 17, when He lifted His eyes toward heaven and said: "Father, the hour has come. Glorify Your Son, that Your Son also may glorify You." (John 17:1).

The Signs (Rushumat) over the Oblation:

  • The First Sign: He says "And gave thanks." That is why we call it the Mystery of the Eucharist (Eucharistia), which is a Greek word meaning "thanksgiving," or we call it the Mystery of Thanksgiving (Luke 22:19).
  • The Second Sign: "And blessed it." Because the Lord Jesus Christ, on the night He instituted the Mystery of Thanksgiving, took bread, blessed it, and broke it (Mark 14:22).
  • The Third Sign: Abouna says "And sanctified it." (John 17:19).

(Each time, the people respond and say: "Amen.")

Signing the Cross three times over the bread and then over the wine is sealing the Oblations (Al-Qarabeen) with the seal of the Cross and consecrating them, because the Cross is the image and seal of Christ. The transformation occurs by the action of the Holy Trinity.

After that, Abouna begins the dividing (Taqseem) of the Oblation (Qurbana). He begins the division gently with the thumb of his right hand from top to bottom, so that one-third of the Oblation is on his right and two-thirds are on his left. He divides it without separating it completely and says: "And He broke it..." (Matthew 26:26).

He opens the Oblation slightly, breathes into it, and says:

"He gave it to His holy disciples and pure apostles, saying: 'Take, eat of it all of you. For this is My Body, which is broken for you and for many, to be given for the remission of sins. This do in remembrance of Me.'" (1 Corinthians 11:24).

He also divides a small section above the Spadikon (Al-Isbadikon), and the same from below the Spadikon. In this way, the Oblation is divided into four sections without separation, forming the shape of the Cross. He places the Lamb in the Paten (Siniya) and rubs his hands together to ensure no particle of the Lamb remains stuck to his hand. Breaking the bread in this manner signifies the sufferings of the Lord Jesus Christ, and placing it in the Paten signifies His burial.

The Explanation of Dividing the Lamb (One-Third Right, Two-Thirds Left):

The First Explanation: The one-third on the right is the inheritance of the Son, who sits at the right hand of Majesty, and the two-thirds are the inheritance of the Father and the Holy Spirit, yet it is one inheritance. The Son, the Second Hypostasis (Oqnum), gives us what is His so that we may reign with Him in the inheritance of the Trinity. He does not separate the one-third from the two-thirds because the inheritance of the Son cannot be separated from the inheritance of the Father and the Holy Spirit; it is one grace and one offering for the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit.

A Second Explanation: The one-third on the right pertains to the Hypostasis of the Only-Begotten Son, who incarnated, died, and rose. He is the one who broke His Body and gave it to His holy disciples, but He did not separate from the Father and the Holy Spirit; that is why the bread remains without separation. The three holes are on the right side. Abouna places his right thumb from top to bottom, representing the mysteries of salvation: the highest is the Birth from the Virgin, the middle is the Baptism in the Jordan, and the lowest is the Death on Golgotha and the Resurrection. (Do you see the beauty of the Liturgy?)

The Divine Liturgy is a real, actual experience: It signifies the Lord Jesus Christ, crucified and risen from the dead, considered slain for us. It is not an event that happened and passed; no, it is a true sacrifice present with us, and a realistic, effective memorial. What we offer is the exact same sacrifice—the Body and Blood of our Lord Jesus Christ [3].

Sanctification of the Chalice (Wine Mixed with Water)

Abouna places his hand on the edge of the Chalice (Kass) and says:

"Likewise also the cup after supper, He mixed it of wine and water."

(This is a Jewish tradition: the first cup must be mixed with water) [4]. The Lord says in the Old Testament: "Come, eat of my bread and drink of the wine I have mixed." (Proverbs 9:5). It is also a memorial of the blood and water that flowed from the side of the Redeemer on the Cross.

Abouna places his finger on the Chalice and moves it from left to right (clockwise). This signifies the factor of time, and that we drink it here in this age and the present world. Then he moves his finger in reverse (counter-clockwise). This signifies what is above time, meaning eternity, and that it is the same cup we will drink in eternity in a spiritual way.

In the same manner, Abouna signs the Chalice with three signs (Rushumat), just like the Lamb: "Gave thanks, blessed it, and sanctified it," with the sign of the Cross.

Then Abouna holds the Chalice in his hand and gently moves it in the shape of a cross, saying:

"He tasted, and gave it to His holy disciples and pure apostles, saying: 'Take, drink of it all of you. For this is My blood of the new covenant which is shed for you and for many, to be given for the remission of sins. This do in remembrance of Me.'" (Matthew 26:27-28).

When Abouna says "He tasted," he breathes the breath of the Holy Spirit, just as he did with the Lamb.

"My blood of the new covenant": This distinguishes it from the Old Covenant, which was based on offering the blood of bulls and goats. All those sacrifices could not and cannot take away sins. Our teacher Paul the Apostle explained this in detail in Hebrews chapter 9. One verse explains this perfectly:

"Not with the blood of goats and calves, but with His own blood He entered the Most Holy Place once for all, having obtained eternal redemption." (Hebrews 9:12).

Moving the Chalice in the shape of a cross signifies that the Lord Jesus Christ shed His blood on the Cross for the salvation of the world. By moving the Chalice in the four directions of the inhabited world, it means He shed His blood for the salvation of the whole world, and He Himself is the propitiation for our sins, and also for the whole world. (1 John 2:2).

The people say: "This is also truly, Amen." This is an affirmation and belief in all of Abouna's prayers. With this, the Blood is sanctified.

The Anamnesis (The Memorial)

After that, Abouna points to the Bread and the Chalice and says:

"For as often as you eat of this Bread and drink of this Cup, you proclaim My death, confess My resurrection, and remember Me until I come." (1 Corinthians 11:26).

What does this mean? It means a real, actual memorial (Zikra Hqiqiyya / Anamnesis) of the presence of the Lord Jesus Christ in the midst of His people through the power of His sufferings [5], His victory, His resurrection, the glory of His ascension, and the joy of His Second Coming.

The people respond and say: "Amen, Amen, Amen. Your death, O Lord, we proclaim. Your holy resurrection and ascension we confess... etc."

This is one of the oldest praises found in the churches and in the liturgies, whether Eastern or Western. This is called the "Sacrifice of Praise," as our teacher Paul the Apostle said: "Therefore by Him let us continually offer the sacrifice of praise to God, that is, the fruit of our lips, giving thanks to His name." (Hebrews 13:15).

Abouna continues and says (pay attention, this is the most important part):

"Now, therefore, as we also commemorate His holy passion, His resurrection from the dead, His ascension into the heavens, His sitting at Your right hand, O Father, and His second coming from the heavens, awesome and full of glory, we offer unto You Your oblations from what is Yours, for every condition, concerning every condition, and in every condition." [6]

The Epiclesis (Calling Down the Holy Spirit)

Here, Abouna prostrates in reverence to pray the Litany of the Descent of the Holy Spirit (Epiclesis), asking the Holy Spirit to descend upon the Oblations (Al-Qarabeen) and transform them.

Abouna says:

"We ask You, O Lord our God, we, Your sinful servants, that Your Holy Spirit may descend upon us and upon these oblations set forth, and purify them, change them, and manifest them as a sanctification of Your saints."

At this moment, the deacon says: "Worship God in fear and trembling."

The whole church must prostrate (Yasjud) as they say: "We praise You, we bless You, we serve You, O Lord, and we worship You." Because in this prayer, Abouna asked for the descent of the Holy Spirit upon us and upon the oblations. This means we are also sanctified along with the oblations! See, if we take these words with deep faith, a pure heart, and a clean mind, believe me, we would all leave the church completely different from before we entered it, because we have been sanctified by the Holy Spirit.

That is why Abouna at the end of the Liturgy, when he says "The Holies for the Holy," says it with assurance, because he asked for our sanctification just as he asked for the sanctification of the oblations. What else do we want?

The deacon here says, "Let us attend. Amen." Meaning, there should be quietness and faith in the church because the Holy Spirit is present on the Altar, sanctifying the oblations and sanctifying us.

At this moment, Abouna rises from prostration and quickly signs the Lamb with three signs (Rushumat), saying:

"And this Bread, a Holy Body of His."

The people respond: "I believe, Amen."

Abouna prostrates and says inaudibly (Sirran):

"Our Lord, God, and Savior Jesus Christ, given for the remission of sins and eternal life to those who partake of Him." (The bread transforms into a true Body).

Abouna rises after the prostration and signs the Chalice (Kass) with three signs, saying aloud:

"And this Cup also, a Precious Blood of His New Covenant." [7]

The people respond and say: "We also believe." (Meaning, we believe in this transformation, whether of the Bread or the Wine).

We notice that Abouna prostrated three times. It is as if he is pleading and crying out to the power and efficacy of the Holy Trinity for the descent of the Holy Spirit and the transformation of the oblations into a true Body and true Blood. This is a handed-down faith. Anyone who entertains doubt is unworthy to approach Communion (Al-Tanawul).

Abouna stands and says:

"Our Lord, God, and Savior Jesus Christ, given for the remission of sins and eternal life to those who partake of Him."

The people and the deacons stand and say "Lord have mercy" (Kyrie Eleison) three times, out of faith in the transformation and a heartfelt belief, untainted by doubt, in the presence of the Body and Blood of the Lord Jesus Christ on the Altar and in our midst.

It is known and understood that the Holy Spirit is the Hypostasis (Oqnum) of Love and the source of gifts. He is the Doer and the Worker in all the mysteries of the Church, as they are accomplished by the work of the Holy Spirit. As the Scripture says: "There are diversities of gifts, but the same Spirit." (1 Corinthians 12:4).

The last thing I will say about the prostration (Sujud) during the descent of the Holy Spirit: Prostration indicates the fall of man and his humiliation. Likewise, rising and standing is a proof of his acceptance before God, his salvation, or the acceptance of his repentance [8]. Prostration indicates the weakness of our nature and man's earthly tendencies. Rising indicates the divine grace by which we are raised, and that He is the One who raises us from our falls.

Abouna prostrating three times is a reference to the Lord Jesus Christ prostrating in the Garden three times, after sanctifying and offering the Lord's Supper (Al-'Asha Al-Rabbani) with His disciples in the Upper Room (Al-'Illiya).

To our God be all glory and honor forever. Amen.
