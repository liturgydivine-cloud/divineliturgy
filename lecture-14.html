The Commemoration of the Departed

Summary of the Previous Lesson:

In the early Church, during the days of the Apostles and for a period afterward, there was no prayer of the Commemoration (Al-Magma') in the sense that we have now in the liturgies of the Coptic Church. However, after the spread of Christianity throughout the inhabited world, and the emergence of saints who had an impact on the history of the Church, preserving the faith, and defending the sanctities of apostolic tradition, the Holy Commemoration began to appear. These names were added to the Liturgy by Ecumenical and local councils.

Over time, the prayer of the Commemoration became what it is today, and the Church continues to add certain saints to the Holy Commemoration, like Pope Kyrillos VI, Archdeacon Habib Girgis, Anba Sarapamon Abu Tarha, the departed Anba Abraam Bishop of Fayoum, and other saints.

The Spiritual Concept of the Prayer of the Holy Commemoration: It is considered a command from the Only-Begotten Son of God, when He asked that the saints be remembered and what they did be told wherever the Gospel is preached. He said:

"Assuredly, I say to you, wherever this gospel is preached in the whole world, what this woman has done will also be told as a memorial to her." (Matthew 26:13).

You know what happened in the house of Simon the Leper: the woman who had the alabaster flask of fragrant oil and poured it on His head. That is why Abouna at the beginning of the Commemoration says: "For this, O Lord, is the command of Your Only-Begotten Son, that we share in the commemoration of Your saints... etc."

The prayer of the Commemoration is a meeting between the Victorious Church in Heaven and the Struggling Church on earth, around the Body and Blood of the Lord Jesus Christ present on the Altar. Meaning, it is a mutual prayer and spiritual cohesion between the invisible Church and the visible Church on earth. A beautiful heavenly scene, because the Church is the earthly Jerusalem.

The prayer of the Struggling Church for them is considered a request to God to increase their repose and rest, and forgive them any spiritual lukewarmness or oversight, even though they finished the race and the crown of righteousness was laid up for them. What confirms this is the prayer of Paul the Apostle for one of his departed loved ones, Onesiphorus, when he said: "The Lord grant to him that he may find mercy from the Lord in that Day." (2 Timothy 1:18).

The commemoration of the saints teaches us to emulate their biography and follow their footsteps in godliness and their love for the Lord Jesus Christ. Because each one of them offered his soul, his body, and his self as an oblation on the altar of the love of Christ. Each one has a love story and a deep, special relationship with God the Father. This in itself benefits us spiritually and pushes us to imitate them, complete our faith, walk in the path of their love, and offer what they offered in their spiritual lives.

The Prayer of the Holy Commemoration begins when Abouna says:

"For this, O Lord, is the command of Your Only-Begotten Son, that we share in the commemoration of Your saints. Graciously accord, O Lord, to remember all the saints who have pleased You since the beginning."

He begins to mention:

  1. Our Holy Fathers the Patriarchs: Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob.
  2. The Prophets: (Referring to the Old Testament prophets from Moses the Prophet to Malachi, without mentioning their specific names).
  3. Our Fathers the Apostles, Preachers, and Evangelists.
  4. The Martyrs and Confessors, and all the spirits of the righteous who were perfected in the faith.

A beautiful spiritual correction [1] occurs when Abouna says:

"And most of all, the Saint full of glory, the ever-virgin Theotokos, the Holy and Pure Mary."

She is the beautiful icon in the Commemoration, and all those who were mentioned are stars around her. Of course, this is a beautiful spiritual correction; she is considered the pinnacle of the Commemoration in a beautiful way that does not diminish those mentioned at the beginning of the Commemoration.

Why does our Mother the Virgin precede all those whom Abouna mentioned at the beginning of the Commemoration?

  • The Virgin is higher than all the heavenly beings, higher than the Archangels.
  • She is the Mother of God, from whom God the Word took flesh.
  • She is the True Queen who sits at the right hand of the King.
  • She is the trusted intercessor before the throne of grace for the human race.

After that, Abouna begins to mention John the Baptist, the Forerunner and Martyr. Then Abouna mentions the Preacher of the Egyptian Lands, Mark, who shed his blood in the streets of Alexandria.

After that, he mentions the Defenders of the Faith who defended the true faith unto blood: He mentions Saint Severus of Antioch, the Holy Father Patriarch Athanasius the Apostolic, the Holy Father Patriarch Anba Dioscorus, and the Seal of the Martyrs, Pope Peter.

Abouna begins to mention holy fathers from other churches who left clear marks in the history of the Church, whether in teaching or faith. Such as Saint Gregory the Theologian (Al-Natiq bil-Ilahiyat), Saint Gregory the Wonderworker, Saint Gregory the Armenian (whom the king threw into a pit for 15 years for refusing to deny his faith in Christ), Saint John Chrysostom (who was exiled from his throne and killed in his exile), and Saint Basil the Great (the author of the Liturgy which our Coptic Church prays with).

He mentions in the Commemoration the three Ecumenical Councils recognized by the Coptic and Oriental Churches, which convened to stand against false teachings and heresies: the Council of Nicaea, the Council of Constantinople, and the Council of Ephesus.

After that, Abouna begins to mention the pillars of monasticism in Egypt and the Christian world. Thus, we find that the Commemoration is truly a beautiful icon of all the saints who were perfected in the faith, headed by our Pure Mother Mary. At the end of the Commemoration, Abouna asks God for mercy when he says: "And all the choirs of Your saints. Through their prayers and supplications, have mercy on us all together, and save us for the sake of Your Holy Name which is called upon us."

The Commemoration of the Departed (Al-Tarheem)

Today, by the grace of God, we will cover the Commemoration of the Departed (Al-Tarheem) in the Divine Liturgy.

The Commemoration of the Departed comes after the prayer of the Assembly of Saints (Al-Magma'), when Abouna prays a secret prayer (Sirran) in which he says:

"Remember also, O Lord, all those who have fallen asleep and reposed in the priesthood, and those in every rank of the laity. Graciously accord, O Lord, to repose all their souls in the bosom of our holy fathers Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. Sustain them in a place of green pasture, by the waters of rest, in the Paradise of Joy, the place from which sorrow, sadness, and sighing have fled away, in the light of Your saints."

The Tarheem is the revival of the memory of our departed fathers and siblings. Therefore, we pray for them, asking for mercy for them; meaning, the Church prays for them, and they pray for us. This is the firm doctrine and faith of the Church regarding the communion of saints and the unity of the remaining and the departed members of the Church. As the Gospel says, our God is the God of the living, not the God of the dead: "For He is not the God of the dead but of the living, for all live to Him." (Luke 20:38). And it says in the Gospel of our teacher Matthew: "I am the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob... God is not the God of the dead, but of the living." (Matthew 22:32).

Because if we lost hope in eternal life, we would be the most miserable of people, and we would say, "Let us eat and drink, for tomorrow we die," as our teacher Paul the Apostle said: "If, in the manner of men, I have fought with beasts at Ephesus, what advantage is it to me? If the dead do not rise, 'Let us eat and drink, for tomorrow we die!'" (1 Corinthians 15:32).

The prayer of the Commemoration of the Departed was mentioned in the Liturgy of Mark the Apostle, which is now called the Liturgy of Saint Cyril (Al-Quddas Al-Kyrillosi). These prayers were transferred to the rest of the Liturgies, but its origin is the Liturgy of Mark the Apostle. And supposedly, the prayer of the Commemoration of the Departed should be for the newly departed, the 40th-day memorial, and the annual memorial, not in every Liturgy [2].

The prayer of the Commemoration of the Departed begins when Abouna says:

"Those, O Lord, and everyone whose names we have mentioned, and those whom we have not mentioned; those who are in the mind of each one of us, and those who are not in our minds; who have fallen asleep and reposed in the faith of Christ."

(Here Abouna begins to mention the names present before him on the Altar, and places incense in the censer).

After that, he continues and says:

"Graciously accord, O Lord, to repose all their souls in the bosom of our holy fathers Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob... etc."

The Conclusion of the Commemoration

After the Tarheem, Abouna prays with his hands raised and says:

"Those, O Lord, whose souls You have taken, repose them in the Paradise of Joy, and we who are sojourners in this place, keep us in Your faith, and grant us Your peace unto the end."

The people respond and say:

"As it was, and shall be, from generation to generation, and unto the ages of all ages. Amen."

Meaning, this is the condition of the Church throughout the generations, since the pure Apostolic Fathers founded it—a life of continuity.

Abouna continues with a beautiful prayer and says:

"And guide us to Your kingdom. That in this, as in all things, Your great and holy Name may be glorified, blessed, and exalted in everything, honored and blessed, with Jesus Christ, Your beloved Son, and the Holy Spirit."

After this prayer, Abouna bows his head toward the priests and deacons and stands to the side of the Altar, so the Paten (Siniya) and the covered Chalice become visible.

He says: "Peace be with all of you," without making the sign of the cross. [3]

Pay attention to this part because it is important. Do you know where this peace is coming from? It is coming from the Lord Jesus Christ. Because Abouna stood to the side, bowed his head, and said this phrase. If everyone does not accept this peace with a heart full of faith, and when the people respond to Abouna saying "And with your spirit," then they are truly just standing in the Liturgy without feeling the meaning of this peace that comes from Christ Himself, whose Body and Blood are present on the Altar.

A Historical Overview of the Prayer of Commemoration (Al-Tarheem):

It is considered one of the ancient prayers found in the liturgies of the Coptic Church and the Eastern Churches. All churches used to write the names of the departed, the requests of the worshipers for the sick and those in distress, and those who offered the oblations (Al-Qarabeen) on two metal tablets written by the deacon. They were called Diptychs (Diptychon / Zibtikhun), which is a Greek word meaning "a two-leaved publication," and they were made of light brass [4].

This system began to disappear in the twentieth century and ended in the Coptic Church in the 1970s. Different types of papers began to appear containing names for the Tarheem, and papers with various requests for the sick and those in distress. (Sometimes, court case files are even placed on the Altar, and in Egypt, seating numbers for exams!)

The scholar Origen, who clearly refers to the remembrance of the dead in a prayer inside the church, says:

"And they reverently make a memorial for the saints who have fallen asleep in the faith, that they may receive comfort and joy, asking that they too may have with them perfection in the faith."

From the Canons of the Church issued by Pope Gabriel Ibn Turayk [5], Canon Number 14 says:

"There are some people who blaspheme the canons and laws of God and perform the Tarheem for those who have fallen asleep on Sunday, which is the day of joy in the Resurrection of our Lord Jesus Christ, seeking empty glory. The canons forbid this and warn against it. Whoever adopts this hereafter is mistaken, and is not in absolution or gain, but in loss and condemnation."

There are people who send Tarheem papers during the Lord's Feasts (Al-A'yad Al-Sayyidiyya). Of course, there is no Tarheem during the Lord's Feasts because it is a day of joy.

And to our God be all glory and honor forever. Amen.

Footnotes

[1] "Spiritual Correction" (Tadaruk Ruhani): A liturgical device where the priest lists great biblical figures and then interrupts himself with "But above all..." to emphasize the Virgin Mary's unique status. ↩
[2] The Frequency of the Tarheem: Traditionally reserved for specific memorial days to avoid somber Sunday overtones, though commonly practiced in every Liturgy today for pastoral reasons. ↩
[3] Peace without the Sign of the Cross: The priest humbles himself because Christ is physically present on the Altar; the peace flows directly from the Sacrament to the people. ↩
[4] Diptychs: Two-hinged tablets listing the names of the living and departed in communion with the church, read during the Anaphora. ↩
[5] Pope Gabriel Ibn Turayk: 70th Pope of Alexandria (12th C.). His canons strictly guarded Sunday as a day of joy, prohibiting somber memorial rites. ↩
